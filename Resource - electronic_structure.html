1. Shells, Sub-shells and Orbitals

Electrons occupy shells (principal energy levels, n = 1, 2, 3…) which are divided into sub-shells.

Sub-shell types

Sub-shell No. of orbitals Max electrons Shape
s 12Spherical
p 36Dumbbell (3 axes)
d 510Complex
f 714Very complex

Three key rules for filling

  • Aufbau principle: fill lowest energy sub-shells first (1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d…)
  • Pauli exclusion principle: each orbital holds at most 2 electrons with opposite spins (↑↓)
  • Hund's rule: electrons occupy orbitals of equal energy singly before pairing
Anomalous configurations (HL must know) Cr (Z=24): [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ — half-filled d is extra stable
Cu (Z=29): [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ — full d is extra stable

2. First Ionisation Energy (IE₁)

Definition The energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms in the ground state, forming one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.
X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻    ΔH = IE₁ (always positive)

General trends

  • Across a period (→): IE₁ generally increases — nuclear charge increases, atomic radius decreases, shielding roughly constant → stronger attraction for outer electrons
  • Down a group (↓): IE₁ decreases — outer electrons are further from nucleus, more shielding from inner shells

Anomalies in Period 2 & 3 — evidence for sub-shells

Anomaly 1 — Group 2 → Group 13 (Be→B, Mg→Al) IE₁(B) < IE₁(Be) even though B has higher Z. B removes a 2p electron (higher energy, more shielded by the 2s²), which is easier to remove than Be's 2s electron.
Anomaly 2 — Group 15 → Group 16 (N→O, P→S) IE₁(O) < IE₁(N) even though O has higher Z. O has a paired 2p electron (↑↓ in one orbital), which experiences extra electron–electron repulsion and is easier to remove. N has unpaired 2p electrons (↑ ↑ ↑) with no pairing repulsion.

3. Successive Ionisation Energies — Evidence for Shell Structure

Remove electrons one by one from the same element and plot the IE for each removal. Sudden large jumps reveal when you cross from one shell to the next.

Na: IE₁=496, IE₂=4562, …, IE₉=28932, IE₁₀=141362, IE₁₁=157980 (kJ mol⁻¹)
  • IE₁ is low → Na has 1 outer electron (valence shell n=3)
  • IE₂ to IE₉ rise gradually → 8 electrons in n=2 shell
  • IE₁₀ is ~5× IE₉ → crossing into n=1 shell (only 2 electrons)
Why use a log scale? Successive IEs span three orders of magnitude. A log₁₀ scale compresses the large values so that jumps within each shell become clearly visible as distinct groups — revealing the sub-shell structure as well as shell boundaries.

Reading sub-shell structure from the log graph of Na

  • IE₁ (3s) — isolated low point
  • IE₂–IE₇ (2p) — cluster of 6 values rising gradually
  • IE₈–IE₉ (2s) — slightly higher cluster of 2 values
  • IE₁₀–IE₁₁ (1s) — massive jump, cluster of 2 very high values

4. First IE Across Z = 1–30 — Evidence for Sub-shells

  • Each new period starts with a sharp drop (e.g., Li, Na, K) — new outer shell, much further from nucleus
  • Noble gases peak each period — full shells, very stable
  • Dips at Group 13 (B, Al) — first 2p/3p electron, screened by 2s/3s
  • Dips at Group 16 (O, S) — first paired p electron, extra repulsion
  • D-block (Sc–Zn): 4s electrons are always removed first in ionisation; the 3d fills but 4s is exposed — relatively flat IE₁ across the d-block

General trend: nuclear charge vs. atomic radius vs. shielding. Two anomalies: (1) Mg→Al drops — think about which sub-shell Al's outer electron occupies. (2) P→S drops — think about electron pairing in the 3p orbital.

Mark Scheme

General trend: IE₁ increases from Na to Ar because nuclear charge increases (Z = 11→18) while shielding from inner electrons remains approximately constant, so the effective nuclear charge increases and outer electrons are more strongly attracted. ✓✓
Anomaly 1 — Mg to Al: IE₁(Al) < IE₁(Mg) despite Al having higher Z. ✓ Al's outer electron is in the 3p sub-shell (3p¹), which is at higher energy and is additionally shielded by the 3s² electrons. ✓ Mg's outer electron (3s²) is in the lower-energy 3s sub-shell with less shielding.
Anomaly 2 — P to S: IE₁(S) < IE₁(P) despite S having higher Z. ✓ S has a paired electron in one 3p orbital (↑↓ ↑ ↑). The electron–electron repulsion in that paired orbital lowers the energy required to remove one of those paired electrons. ✓ P has three unpaired 3p electrons (↑ ↑ ↑ — Hund's rule), with no pairing repulsion. ✓

5. Chromium (Cr, Z=24) and copper (Cu, Z=29) have electron configurations that deviate from the expected Aufbau order. 7 marks

(a) Write the expected and actual configurations of Cr and Cu. [4]
(b) Explain why the actual configurations are more stable. [2]
(c) Suggest why the first IE of Cu (745 kJ mol⁻¹) is higher than that of Ni (737 kJ mol⁻¹) but lower than that of Zn (906 kJ mol⁻¹). [3]

For Cr: expected is [Ar]3d⁴4s², actual is [Ar]3d⁵4s¹. For Cu: expected [Ar]3d⁹4s², actual [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹. The stability comes from half-filled and fully-filled d sub-shells. For part (c) think about what is being removed in each case and what Zn's 3d¹⁰ does.

Mark Scheme

(a) Cr expected: [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s² ; actual: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ ✓✓
Cu expected: [Ar] 3d⁹ 4s² ; actual: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ ✓✓
(b) A half-filled d sub-shell (3d⁵, Cr) and a fully-filled d sub-shell (3d¹⁰, Cu) have extra stability due to the symmetrical distribution of electron density and exchange energy. ✓ The energy gained from achieving these configurations outweighs the cost of promoting a 4s electron to 3d. ✓
(c) Cu (3d¹⁰ 4s¹): the single 4s¹ electron is removed. This is slightly higher than Ni because increasing Z gives higher effective nuclear charge on the outer 4s electron. ✓ Zn (3d¹⁰ 4s²) has IE₁ much higher because the 3d¹⁰ provides less effective shielding of the 4s² pair (d electrons shield poorly compared to s/p), and the 4s² electrons experience a higher effective nuclear charge. ✓ Also, Zn's 3d subshell is fully filled and contributes to a contraction of the 4s orbital, raising IE₁. ✓
