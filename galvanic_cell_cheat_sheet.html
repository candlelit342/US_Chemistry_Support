1. The key ideas, in brief

What a galvanic (voltaic) cell is
A galvanic cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy via a spontaneous redox reaction. It is built from two half-cells — each a metal electrode dipped in a solution of its own ions — connected by an external wire (through which electrons flow) and a salt bridge (through which ions flow to keep both solutions electrically neutral). Separating the oxidation and reduction half-reactions into two half-cells forces the electrons to travel through the external circuit, which is what lets you extract useful electrical work (and measure a voltage) instead of the electrons just transferring directly on contact, as they would if the two metals touched in one beaker.
Oxidation and reduction — which electrode is which
Oxidation is loss of electrons; reduction is gain of electrons. Remember this with OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.
In any galvanic cell: AN OX — oxidation happens at the ANode. RED CAT — reduction happens at the CAThode.
The anode is the electrode that gives up electrons into the external circuit (it is the source of electrons, so conventionally it is the negative terminal in a galvanic cell). The cathode receives those electrons and is where reduction occurs (the positive terminal in a galvanic cell).
Standard electrode potential, E°
Every half-cell has an intrinsic "pulling power" for electrons, measured (relative to the standard hydrogen electrode, defined as 0.00 V) as its standard electrode potential, E°, always written as a reduction potential. A more positive E° means a greater tendency to be reduced (to gain electrons); a more negative E° means a greater tendency to be oxidised (to lose electrons). In a cell built from two half-cells, the one with the more positive E° becomes the cathode, and the one with the more negative E° becomes the anode.
Cell potential (EMF)
The cell's electromotive force is the difference between the two electrode potentials: Ecell = Ecathode − Eanode. Under standard conditions (1.00 M, 298 K) this becomes E°cell. A positive Ecell means the reaction is spontaneous as written (which is why a correctly wired galvanic cell always gives a positive reading).
Why concentration and temperature matter — the Nernst equation
E° values are defined only at standard conditions (1.00 M, 298 K). Away from standard conditions, the actual electrode potential shifts according to the Nernst equation: E = E° + (0.0592/n)·log₁₀[Mn+] at 298 K, where n is the number of electrons transferred in that half-equation. Raising the ion concentration raises E (Le Chatelier's Principle — see below); lowering it lowers E. Temperature affects E° itself, roughly linearly, via each electrode's own empirical temperature coefficient, dE°/dT.
Le Chatelier's Principle and Kc applied to a half-cell
Treat a half-equation such as Mn+(aq) + n e⁻ ⇌ M(s) as a reversible equilibrium. Increasing [Mn+] shifts this equilibrium to the right (towards the metal), i.e. it increases the half-cell's tendency to be reduced — so its electrode potential becomes more positive (or less negative). Decreasing [Mn+] shifts the equilibrium left, decreasing the tendency to be reduced, so the potential becomes more negative. This is exactly the same direction of shift you'd predict from the reaction quotient, Q, in the Nernst equation: Q gets smaller as [Mn+] decreases, which — because of the minus-log relationship — makes E increase relative to when Q is larger. Both viewpoints (equilibrium shift and Q) agree; use whichever explanation the question is asking for.
Concentration cells
If both half-cells use the same metal, E°cell = 0 (there's no difference in standard potential — both half-cells contain the same species). Any EMF that appears must come entirely from a difference in concentration between the two sides. The more concentrated half-cell has the higher (more positive) electrode potential, so it becomes the cathode; the more dilute half-cell becomes the anode. This is a "concentration cell" and is a direct, clean test of the Nernst equation on its own, with no E° term to complicate things.
Systematic (zero) error
A real analogue meter's needle can rest slightly away from true zero even with no potential difference applied — a "zero error". This is a systematic error: it shifts every reading by a constant amount in the same direction, and averaging repeated readings will not remove it (unlike random error). It must be corrected by re-zeroing the instrument (e.g. with the meter's zero-adjustment screw) before taking measurements, or accounted for by subtracting the offset afterwards.

2. Vocabulary and definitions

TermDefinition
Galvanic (voltaic) cellA device that converts the chemical energy of a spontaneous redox reaction into electrical energy.
ElectrodeA solid conductor (usually a metal) at which a half-reaction takes place; also used loosely to mean the whole half-cell.
ElectrolyteAn ionic solution that conducts electricity via the movement of ions.
Half-cellOne electrode dipped in its own electrolyte solution — one half of a complete galvanic cell.
Salt bridgeA tube of inert electrolyte (e.g. saturated KNO₃ in agar gel) connecting the two half-cells, allowing ions to migrate and keep both solutions electrically neutral without letting the solutions mix directly.
AnodeThe electrode at which oxidation occurs. In a galvanic cell it is the negative terminal and the source of electrons for the external circuit.
CathodeThe electrode at which reduction occurs. In a galvanic cell it is the positive terminal, and electrons flow into it from the external circuit.
OxidationLoss of electrons by a species (its oxidation number increases).
ReductionGain of electrons by a species (its oxidation number decreases).
Redox reactionA reaction in which oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously — electrons transfer from one species to another.
Oxidising agent (oxidant)The species that causes another species to be oxidised. In doing so, the oxidising agent itself is reduced.
Reducing agent (reductant)The species that causes another species to be reduced. In doing so, the reducing agent itself is oxidised.
Half-equationAn equation showing only the oxidation or only the reduction part of a redox reaction, including the electrons transferred explicitly.
Standard hydrogen electrode (SHE)The reference half-cell (H⁺(aq, 1.00 M) | H₂(g, 1 atm) | Pt) defined to have E° = 0.00 V exactly, against which all other standard electrode potentials are measured.
Standard electrode potential, E°The potential of a half-cell measured against the SHE under standard conditions (1.00 M, 298 K, 1 atm), always expressed as a reduction potential.
Electromotive force (EMF) / cell potential, EcellThe potential difference between the cathode and the anode of a complete cell; the "driving force" of the cell reaction, Ecell = Ecathode − Eanode.
Nernst equationThe equation describing how electrode (or cell) potential varies with concentration (and, via E°, with temperature): E = E° + (0.0592/n)·log₁₀[ion] at 298 K.
Reaction quotient, QAn expression with the same form as the equilibrium constant, but calculated from the concentrations present at any moment (not necessarily at equilibrium). Used inside the full Nernst equation.
Equilibrium constant, KcThe value of the reaction quotient once a reversible reaction has reached equilibrium — describes the position of that equilibrium at a given temperature.
Le Chatelier's PrincipleIf a system at equilibrium is disturbed (e.g. by changing a concentration), the equilibrium shifts in the direction that partially opposes the disturbance.
Concentration cellA galvanic cell in which both half-cells contain the same metal/ion couple but at different concentrations; E°cell = 0, so any EMF arises purely from the concentration difference.
Spontaneous reactionA reaction that proceeds on its own without continuous external energy input; corresponds to a positive Ecell (and a negative ΔG).
Inert electrodeAn electrode (e.g. platinum or graphite) that conducts electrons but does not itself take part in the redox reaction — used when neither half-reaction involves a solid metal.
Cell notation (cell diagram)A shorthand way of writing a cell: anode | anode electrolyte || cathode electrolyte | cathode, read left to right in the direction electrons flow through the external circuit, anode always written first.
Systematic (zero) errorA consistent offset error affecting every reading in the same way and direction; not reduced by repeating/averaging measurements, and must be corrected at the instrument or in the analysis.

3. Phraseology — correct usage in redox and electrochemistry

These phrases are frequently used loosely or backwards. Use the "correct usage" column as your model.

Phrase / situationCorrect usage and explanation
"X is oxidised" Means X loses electrons (X's oxidation number increases). Do not say "X oxidises" to mean this — "X oxidises Y" means X causes Y to lose electrons (X is the oxidising agent).
"X is reduced" Means X gains electrons (X's oxidation number decreases). Avoid confusing this with "X reduces Y", which means X causes Y to gain electrons (X is the reducing agent).
"X is the oxidising agent" X is the species that is itself reduced, and in being reduced it removes electrons from (oxidises) something else. A common error is to say the oxidising agent "is oxidised" — it is not; it causes oxidation elsewhere while being reduced itself.
"X is the reducing agent" X is the species that is itself oxidised, and in being oxidised it supplies electrons to (reduces) something else.
Writing a half-equation Always show the electrons explicitly on the correct side. Reduction: Mn+(aq) + n e⁻ → M(s) (electrons on the reactant side). Oxidation: M(s) → Mn+(aq) + n e⁻ (electrons on the product side). E° tables are conventionally written as reductions — reverse the equation (and the sign of E°) to get the oxidation half-equation.
"Electrode potential" vs "cell potential" An electrode (half-cell) potential, E, describes one half-cell alone (always relative to the SHE). A cell potential, Ecell, describes the potential difference of a whole cell made of two half-cells: Ecell = Ecathode − Eanode. Do not use these terms interchangeably.
Sign of E° and spontaneity A larger (more positive) E° means a stronger tendency to be reduced, i.e. a stronger oxidising agent. A larger (more negative) E° means a stronger tendency to be oxidised, i.e. a stronger reducing agent. Never describe a negative E° as meaning "no reaction happens" — it simply means that half-reaction, as a reduction, is unfavourable; it can still proceed as an oxidation.
Anode/cathode vs positive/negative terminal In a galvanic cell (the type in this activity): anode = negative terminal, cathode = positive terminal. Be aware this assignment reverses in an electrolytic cell (one driven by an external power supply), where the cathode becomes the negative terminal. Always specify which type of cell you mean.
"The reaction shifts towards the products/reactants" Preferred phrasing over vague terms like "moves". State explicitly which direction (left/right, or towards products/reactants) and link it to the disturbance that caused it, per Le Chatelier's Principle.
Describing a concentration change's effect on potential Say "the electrode potential becomes more positive/less negative" or "more negative/less positive" rather than just "increases/decreases" — this avoids ambiguity when a value moves from, e.g., −0.80 V towards −0.76 V (technically an increase, but easy to misread).
Common mix-up: students often write "zinc is reduced" when they mean "zinc reduces copper ions" (i.e. zinc is the reducing agent and is itself oxidised). Always double-check which species is gaining electrons (reduced) and which is losing them (oxidised, and therefore the reducing agent) before describing a reaction.
