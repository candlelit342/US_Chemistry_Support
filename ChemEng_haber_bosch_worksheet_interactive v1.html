Background

Ammonia (NH₃) is the basis of all nitrogen fertilisers, which feed roughly half the global population. It is produced by the Haber-Bosch process. Because the reaction is exothermic and results in a decrease in moles of gas, the choice of temperature and pressure in the Converter strongly affects both the equilibrium yield and the rate of reaction.

Q1

Using Le Chatelier's Principle, explain why increasing temperature decreases the equilibrium conversion.

💡 What to think about

The forward reaction is exothermic — it releases heat. Think of heat as a "product" of the reaction. What does Le Chatelier say happens when you add more of a product?

📚 Key principle

Le Chatelier's Principle: if a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to oppose the change. Adding heat (raising T) is like adding a product → equilibrium shifts left (reverse reaction) → less NH₃ forms.

"Because the forward reaction is exothermic, increasing temperature causes the equilibrium to shift to the left because..."
Q2

Describe what happens to the Rate Index as temperature increases, and explain why, using collision theory.

💡 What to think about

For a reaction to happen, particles must collide AND have enough energy. The minimum energy needed is called the activation energy (Eₐ).

📚 Key fact

As temperature rises: (1) particles move faster → more frequent collisions, and (2) a greater proportion of particles have kinetic energy ≥ Eₐ → more successful collisions per second → faster rate. The activation energy for NH₃ synthesis on iron is about 59 kJ mol⁻¹.

✏️ Sentence starter
"The Rate Index increases as temperature rises because higher temperatures give particles more kinetic energy, meaning a greater proportion of collisions have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy, so..."

Q5

Using Le Chatelier's Principle, explain why increasing pressure increases the equilibrium conversion. Your answer must refer to the moles of gas on each side of the equation.

💡 Count the moles of gas

Write out:   N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
Left side: 1 + 3 = 4 moles of gas
Right side: 2 moles of gas
Increasing pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gas.

📚 Le Chatelier for pressure

Increasing pressure compresses the system. The equilibrium shifts to reduce the pressure — i.e., to the side with fewer moles of gas. Here that is the right-hand side (more NH₃).

Q6

How does pressure affect the Rate Index in the simulator? Suggest a reason for this trend.

💡 What to think about

Higher pressure means the same number of gas molecules is squeezed into a smaller volume. What does this do to how often molecules collide with each other — and with the catalyst surface?

📚 Key idea

Higher pressure → higher concentration of reactants → more frequent collisions per second → greater rate. For surface catalysis on iron, higher partial pressures mean more N₂ and H₂ molecules adsorbing onto the catalyst surface per second.

Q7

If both equilibrium conversion and rate improve with pressure, why is the process not run at 800 or 1000 atm? Give at least two practical reasons.

💡 Think about the engineering

Consider what it takes to build and operate equipment at 1000 atm. Think about: the cost of building and running compressors, the strength requirements for pipework and reaction vessels, and the consequences of a failure at that pressure.

📚 Key practical limits
  • Cost: Compressing gas to very high pressures requires enormous energy and very expensive equipment.
  • Safety: Vessels and pipework must withstand extreme pressure; failure risk increases; hydrogen causes "embrittlement" of steel.
  • Diminishing returns: Going from 200→300 atm gives a large conversion gain; 900→1000 atm gives very little extra benefit.

Q12

A small portion of the recycled gas (~15%) is continuously removed as a "purge stream" rather than being recycled. Suggest why this purge is necessary.

💡 What's in the feedstocks?

Air contains approximately 78% N₂, 21% O₂, but also about 1% argon (Ar) and traces of other gases. Natural gas is mostly CH₄ but always contains some impurities. What happens to these inert gases as they go round and round the recycle loop?

📚 The problem

Argon, methane, and other inerts don't react in the Converter. Without a purge, they would build up in the recycle loop over time, diluting the H₂ and N₂, reducing their partial pressures, and slowing the reaction rate. The purge removes them before they accumulate.

Q14

An iron catalyst (with K₂O and Al₂O₃ promoters) is used in the Converter. The catalyst does not appear in your equilibrium conversion results. Explain what the catalyst does affect, and why its use allows a lower operating temperature to be chosen.

💡 Catalyst vs equilibrium

A catalyst affects the rate of a reaction, not the position of equilibrium. It provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy (Eₐ). The equilibrium conversion at any given T and P is the same with or without a catalyst.

📚 The knock-on effect

Without catalyst: need ~600 °C to get an acceptable rate → but at 600 °C equilibrium conversion is very low.
With iron catalyst (Eₐ ≈ 59 kJ/mol): acceptable rate achieved at ~400–450 °C → equilibrium conversion is much higher at this lower temperature.
Catalyst → lower T → better equilibrium → higher conversion per pass.

The Haber-Bosch process accounts for ~1–2% of global energy consumption. Using what you now know about the conditions required, suggest two ways engineers might reduce its energy demand in the future.

💡 Where does the energy go?

Think about which parts of the process use the most energy: (1) making H₂ from natural gas, (2) compressing gases to 200+ atm, (3) heating the Converter to 400–500 °C. Which of these could be made more efficient or done differently?

📚 Ideas to consider
  • Better catalysts (e.g. ruthenium-based) that work at lower T and P → less compression and heating energy needed.
  • Green hydrogen: replace natural gas with electrolysis using renewable electricity → eliminates CO₂ emissions from H₂ production.
  • Electrochemical synthesis: convert N₂ to NH₃ using electricity directly at room temperature and pressure (currently being researched).
  • Better heat integration: use the heat released by the exothermic reaction to pre-heat incoming gases, reducing external energy input.
