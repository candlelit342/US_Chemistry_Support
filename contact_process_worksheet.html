▶ Model answers
Turbo-blower: Forces large volumes of air into the system at controlled flow rate / needed to push gas through the whole plant against pipe resistance.
Drying tower: Removes water vapour from incoming air using concentrated H₂SO₄ as desiccant / water poisons the V₂O₅ catalyst and causes acid mist in the absorber.
Melter-settler: Heats solid sulfur with steam to produce molten sulfur; the settler section allows ash and impurities to settle out / molten sulfur burns far more efficiently and the impurity removal protects the catalyst.
Sulfur burner: Combusts molten sulfur in dried air → SO₂ gas / converts the raw material into the key feedstock for the converter.
Waste heat boiler: Recovers heat from the hot SO₂ gas (~1000 °C) to raise steam / reduces energy costs; the gas must also be cooled to converter inlet temperature.
Hot gas filter: Removes dust and particulate matter from the SO₂ gas stream / particles would poison the V₂O₅ catalyst and cause pressure drop.
Gas-gas heat exchanger: Uses hot converter exit gas to pre-heat the converter feed gas / pre-heating reduces fuel costs and brings the feed gas to catalyst light-off temperature without external heating.
4-pass converter: Catalytically oxidises SO₂ to SO₃ over V₂O₅ in four stages with inter-bed cooling / four passes allow high overall conversion while maintaining near-optimal temperature in each bed.
Economiser: Uses residual heat in converter exit gas to pre-heat boiler feedwater / further heat recovery improves overall energy efficiency.
Oleum tower: Absorbs SO₃ into concentrated H₂SO₄ (≥98%) forming oleum (H₂S₂O₇) / SO₃ cannot be absorbed in water directly as this produces a corrosive acid mist.
Absorbing tower: Absorbs remaining SO₃ into 98% H₂SO₄ to give the product acid / completes absorption of SO₃ to maximise yield and prevent tail gas pollution.
Question 2
Why must the air be dried before it enters the sulfur burner? Give two reasons.
💡 Hint
Think about what water vapour would do (a) in the converter and (b) at the absorber. What does water plus SO₃ produce?
▶ Model answer
1. Water vapour poisons (deactivates) the V₂O₅ catalyst in the converter, reducing catalytic activity and shortening catalyst life.
2. If SO₃ contacts water vapour in the gas phase it forms sulphuric acid mist — tiny aerosol droplets that do not coalesce in the absorber and pass out through the exit stack as pollution. Drying prevents this mist formation.
Question 3
SO₃ is not absorbed directly in water to produce H₂SO₄. Explain why, and describe what is done instead.
💡 Hint
Consider what state sulphuric acid is in when it first forms from SO₃ and water at typical process temperatures. Why is an aerosol a problem?
▶ Model answer
If SO₃ contacts liquid water it reacts so vigorously that the heat of reaction vaporises the water, producing a fine mist of sulphuric acid droplets that are too small to be collected and escape to atmosphere as a pollutant.

Instead, SO₃ is first absorbed into 98% sulphuric acid (not 100%) in the oleum and absorbing towers. In the oleum tower it dissolves into the acid to form oleum (fuming sulphuric acid, H₂S₂O₇). The oleum is then diluted with carefully controlled amounts of water, or blended with weaker acid, to give 98% H₂SO₄ without misting.
Question 4
The simulation shows that the hot gas after the sulfur burner travels through the waste heat boiler and gas-gas heat exchanger before reaching the converter. Suggest two economic benefits of this heat management.
▶ Model answer
1. The waste heat boiler converts thermal energy from combustion into steam, which can be used to power turbines elsewhere in the plant or to heat the melter-settler — reducing external fuel costs.
2. The gas-gas heat exchanger pre-heats the cold converter feed gas using the hot converter exit gas. This reduces (or eliminates) the need to externally heat the feed gas to reach catalyst operating temperature (~430 °C), saving energy costs.

(b) Explain your answer using Le Chatelier's Principle:
💡 Hint
Le Chatelier: when temperature is increased, the equilibrium shifts in the direction that absorbs heat. Which direction is that for an exothermic reaction?
▶ Model answer
(a) B is correct.

(b) The forward reaction (SO₂ → SO₃) is exothermic (ΔH = −197 kJ mol⁻¹). By Le Chatelier's Principle, increasing temperature favours the endothermic (reverse) direction to oppose the change. Therefore the equilibrium shifts to the left, producing less SO₃ at equilibrium. The equilibrium constant Kp decreases as temperature increases — you can observe this in the simulation.

Question 8
The converter operates at about 430–490 °C. At this temperature the equilibrium yield is not at its theoretical maximum, yet it is chosen deliberately. Explain the reasoning, considering both rate and yield.
💡 Hint
Rate of reaction increases with temperature (Arrhenius equation / activation energy). Equilibrium yield decreases with temperature (Question 6). What happens at very low T? At very high T?
▶ Model answer
At low temperatures (e.g. <300 °C): the equilibrium yield would be very high, but the reaction rate would be very slow — the catalyst is not sufficiently active, and the rate of equilibration would be too slow for an economic plant.

At high temperatures (e.g. >600 °C): the reaction is fast but the equilibrium position shifts left, so the yield of SO₃ is low. Also catalyst sintering may occur.

The compromise temperature of 430–490 °C gives a sufficiently fast rate (catalyst is active and kinetics are favourable) while maintaining an acceptable equilibrium yield (~97%). This is the temperature that maximises the rate of production of SO₃ rather than the equilibrium yield alone.
Question 9 — The V₂O₅ Catalyst
(a) What is the role of the vanadium(V) oxide catalyst? Does it change the equilibrium yield of SO₃?
(b) Name two substances that can poison (deactivate) the V₂O₅ catalyst, and explain how the plant design guards against one of them.
▶ Model answer
(a) The catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy, increasing the rate of approach to equilibrium. It does not change the position of equilibrium or the equilibrium yield — it only affects how quickly equilibrium is reached.

(b) Poisons include: water/steam; dust and ash particles; arsenic compounds (from impure sulfur); fluorides.
The plant guards against water by passing air through the drying tower (98% H₂SO₄ desiccant) before it enters the process. Dust and ash are removed by the hot gas filter downstream of the waste heat boiler.
Question 10 — The Equilibrium Constant Kp
(a) Write the expression for Kp for:   2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g)
(b) The simulation shows Kp ≈ 190 atm⁻¹ at 450 °C. At 550 °C, would you expect Kp to be larger or smaller? Check this using the simulation, then explain using thermodynamics.
💡 Hint
Recall: ΔG° = −RT ln K. How does ΔG° change with T for an exothermic reaction? Or think about the van't Hoff equation: d(ln K)/dT = ΔH/(RT²).
▶ Model answer
(a) Kp = p²(SO₃) / [p²(SO₂) × p(O₂)]   (units: atm⁻¹, or Pa⁻¹ if SI partial pressures used)

(b) Kp will be smaller at 550 °C than at 450 °C. Since the reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0), the van't Hoff equation gives d(ln Kp)/dT = ΔH/RT² < 0, meaning Kp decreases as T increases. You can verify this in the simulation: at 550 °C, Kp ≈ 13 atm⁻¹, much less than 190 atm⁻¹ at 450 °C, consistent with a lower equilibrium yield.

Question 13 — The 4-Pass Converter
The converter has four catalyst beds with air injected between the beds to cool the gas. Explain why this design achieves higher conversion than a single large catalyst bed would at the same average temperature.
💡 Hint
Remember: the reaction is exothermic. What happens to the gas temperature as it passes through a catalyst bed? How does this affect equilibrium yield as the gas moves through the bed?
▶ Model answer
The converter reaction is strongly exothermic (−197 kJ mol⁻¹). As gas passes through a single bed, the reaction releases heat and the gas temperature rises. Because higher temperature shifts the equilibrium to the left (Le Chatelier), the maximum achievable conversion falls as the gas heats up. A long single bed would reach a temperature where the equilibrium yield is poor.

With four passes: the gas enters each bed cooled to near the optimum temperature (~430–450 °C). As it passes through the bed, it heats up and reacts — achieving partial conversion close to the equilibrium value at that temperature. Between beds, air injection cools the gas back towards 430–450 °C, resetting the equilibrium so the next bed can achieve further conversion. Each successive bed achieves incrementally more conversion, with the overall result approaching very high total conversion (~97–99%) that would be impossible in a single bed.

💡 Hint
Atom economy = (Mr of desired product / total Mr of all reactants) × 100 %. Are there any by-products in the overall equation?
▶ Model answer
Total Mr of reactants = 32 + 32 + 18 = 82. Mr of H₂SO₄ = 98.   Hmm — but 32+32+18 = 82 ≠ 98. Let's check atom balance: S(32)+O₂(32)+H₂O(18) = 82 on the left, but H₂SO₄ = 2+32+64 = 98. This checks out because the atoms are conserved: S→S, 2O from O₂ go to SO₄, 2O from H₂O go to SO₄, and 2H go to H₂SO₄ giving 4O total in SO₄ ✓. So atom economy = 98/82 × 100 — wait, that gives >100%! This is because the convention for atom economy uses Mr of desired products / Mr of all reactants × 100 and the equation must balance. Let's use the balanced masses: LHS 32+32+18=82g mol⁻¹ total → 82g reactant → 98g H₂SO₄? No, that can't be right.

Correction: The equation S + O₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ already balances perfectly (S:1, O:3+1=4 on left from O₂ and H₂O, giving SO₄; H:2). There are no by-products, so Atom Economy = 100%. This is because every atom from the reactants ends up in the desired product H₂SO₄.

A 100% atom economy is ideal from a green chemistry perspective — it means there is no waste in the theoretical reaction stoichiometry. All atoms from the feedstocks (S, air, water) end up in the product.

🌍 Section E: Industrial & Environmental Considerations
Question 21 — Tail Gas SO₂
Even at 97% conversion, 0.94 kmol hr⁻¹ of SO₂ remains unconverted and exits through the absorbing tower as tail gas.

(a) Calculate the mass flow of SO₂ in the tail gas (Mr(SO₂) = 64 g mol⁻¹):
ṁ(SO₂) tail = kg hr⁻¹
(b) Why is SO₂ in the tail gas an environmental problem? Name the phenomenon it contributes to and describe its effects.
(c) Modern plants use double absorption (also called the double contact process). Describe how this works and why it reduces SO₂ emissions.
▶ Model answer
(a) 0.9375 kmol hr⁻¹ × 64 = 60 kg hr⁻¹ SO₂ in tail gas.

(b) SO₂ dissolves in atmospheric water to form sulphurous acid (H₂SO₃) and is further oxidised to sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), causing acid rain. Acid rain damages stone buildings and statues (especially limestone/marble, which reacts with H₂SO₄), acidifies lakes and rivers (harming aquatic life), damages forests, and corrodes metals. SO₂ itself is also a respiratory irritant.

(c) In double absorption (double contact), gas is taken out of the converter after the 3rd catalyst pass and sent through an intermediate absorber to remove the SO₃ already formed. The remaining SO₂-rich gas is then returned for the 4th pass. Removing the SO₃ product shifts the equilibrium of 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ to the right (Le Chatelier), allowing the 4th pass to achieve a much higher additional conversion. Overall, total SO₂ conversion rises from ~97% to ~99.8%, dramatically reducing tail gas SO₂ and acid rain contribution.

Question 23 — Uses and Importance of Sulphuric Acid
Sulphuric acid is sometimes called the "lifeblood of industry". Research or recall three major industrial uses of H₂SO₄ and state why it is needed in each case.
Industrial useWhy H₂SO₄ is needed
▶ Model answers (examples)
1. Fertiliser manufacture — H₂SO₄ reacts with phosphate rock to make superphosphate and with ammonia to make ammonium sulphate fertiliser; both supply essential plant nutrients (P, N).
2. Pickling of steel — dilute H₂SO₄ dissolves surface iron oxides (rust/scale) from steel sheet before galvanising, coating, or further processing.

Other valid uses: dyes and pigments manufacturing, petrochemical refining, pharmaceutical synthesis, explosives (nitration), water treatment.
Question 24 — Extension: Sustainable Development
The Contact Process has been refined over more than a century. Suggest three improvements a chemical engineer might investigate to make a future sulphuric acid plant more sustainable (consider energy, raw materials, and emissions).
▶ Suggested ideas (many valid answers)
1. Better catalysts: A more active catalyst (e.g. caesium-promoted V₂O₅) could operate efficiently at lower temperatures (~380 °C), increasing the equilibrium yield and reducing energy consumption.
2. Waste sulfur feedstocks: Using recovered sulfur from oil refinery desulfurisation (the Claus process) instead of mined elemental sulfur — turning waste into feedstock.
3. Double-absorption as standard: Mandatory use of double contact/double absorption to reduce SO₂ tail gas to <0.01%, minimising acid rain contribution.
4. Better heat integration: Advanced process integration (pinch analysis) to maximise heat recovery and minimise external energy input, possibly allowing the plant to export net electricity.
5. Tail gas scrubbing: Adding an ammonia scrubber on the exit stack to capture residual SO₂ as ammonium sulphate — which can be sold as fertiliser.
