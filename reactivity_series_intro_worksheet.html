Getting Started — Reactivity Series & Cell EMF

This introductory worksheet gets you exploring the galvanic cell simulator, links what you find to the Reactivity Series of metals, and shows you that the cell EMF can be predicted from a table of numbers — not just observed. Work through each stage, answer the self-marked questions as you go (click “Check” after each one), and generate your progress report at the end.

The apparatus

Objectives

This interactive is introduced after you have already had some practical experience of creating your own batteries from half-cells. Nothing replaces the delight of hands-on, minds-on laboratory work, and writing down your observations and discussing them with other students and your teacher.

The purpose of this interactive is to enable you to consolidate these ideas, and even to enable you to try out some half-cells with exotic metals that would not be considered safe in the ‘open laboratory’. Some of your teachers may have used these metals in their research careers. Learning is socially constructed, and it is important that you are able to develop the fluency to discuss these ideas, with the right vocabulary terms and terminology. We use vertical whiteboards to build up student confidence in “drawing while talking”. This engenders a ‘can-do’ approach when encountering problem-solving in a wide range of contexts, whether the laboratory or the Exam Hall.

I wrote this interactive to help students get more curious about this ‘battery’ phenomenon, and to look ‘under the bonnet’ at the seemingly different effects of changing electrolyte concentration and changing the temperature. For a chosen ‘system’ of two half-cells, changing either concentration or temperature opens up the avenues of delightful scientific exploration. You can explore and plan a ‘dummy’ research project using this interactive. The supporting worksheets will support you in guiding your approach and your analysis. It will enable you to quickly plot graphs and draw lines of best fit. You will measure gradients and use these gradients to come to some mathematical conclusions. For the change of concentration, you are exploring what is known as the Nernst Equation. For the change of temperature the analysis is analogous to the Arrhenius Plot you will have carried out in your study of Kinetics, and is known as a Van ’t Hoff Plot instead! Whilst the Arrhenius Plot gave you a gradient that gave you a handle on the Activation Energy, the Van ’t Hoff plot will give you a handle on the Entropy Change! You can carry out exciting laboratory IA projects, with all you need for a top-mark research paper as a submission. Analysis, if it is to be of high quality is painstaking, and many students find that achieving the depth of analysis and evaluation all the way through is a daunting prospect, especially if you have seen complete IA research papers. But like all research, it is building things up stepwise, and these worksheets are here to support your development and your thinking as you also conduct your own experiments and write your Paper.

As such this Interactive is intended to be your “Laboratory Assistant” here to guide you and give you a clear direction of where you are heading.

Getting started

  • Use the interactive galvanic cell interactive below throughout this worksheet.
  • Choose two different electrodes with the pull-down menus.
  • Keep both concentrations at 1.00 M and both temperatures at 25°C for now — you are only investigating the effect of changing the metals.
  • Close the circuit. By default it should be closed initially, and there is a switch located along the black electrical wire just above where it passes the ‘salt bridge’ label.
  • Try changing the metals and writing on paper the different EMF you can record using the analogue scale.
  • Being able to read analogue scales is a key scientific skill. You will encounter them in the laboratory, in industry and in your research careers.
  • To help you check your accuracy, at any time you can click the meter’s “V” (or the “Show reading” button below the meter) to reveal the EMF as a digital approximation, which might be more precise than your interpretation of the analogue reading.

Safety

If you are following this up in the laboratory, remember that these metal salt solutions are often toxic, and some are serious eye corrosives, so Eye Protection must be worn throughout.

Tip: the analogue needle may have a small built-in “zero error” — use the screwdriver button once at the start to correct it, just as you would zero a real meter before taking readings.

Stage 1

Explore: which pair of metals gives the biggest EMF?

Try different combinations of electrodes. Which pair gives you the highest cell EMF?

1. Leave concentrations at 1.00 M and temperatures at 25°C for both electrodes.
2. Choose a Left and a Right electrode, close the circuit, and reveal the reading.
3. Record at least five different pairs and their EMF in the table below — include some pairs you expect to be small and some you expect to be large.
4. Keep changing the pair, always trying to beat your own highest EMF so far, until you are confident you have found the best possible pair from the eight metals shown by default (don’t turn on “More exotic metals” yet — save that for later exploration).
5. Note: the digital reading only shows the size of the reading with a “+” when it is a normal (non-reversed) connection — if your reading is negative, try swapping which metal is on the left and which is on the right.

Stage 2

Spot the pattern on the Reactivity Series

Open the Reactivity Series page in a new tab. Find the two metals that gave you the highest EMF in Stage 1, and look at where they sit in the list.

Q3. On the Reactivity Series list, the two metals that give the highest EMF are…

In your own words: why do you think metals that are further apart on the Reactivity Series give a bigger cell EMF?

Use the vocabulary from the Ideas / Vocabulary sheet: think about how strongly each metal “wants” to lose electrons (be oxidised) versus how strongly the other metal’s ion “wants” to gain electrons (be reduced), and what happens to the overall driving force when those two tendencies are as different as possible.
Stage 3

Back to basics: the zinc–copper cell

The “classic” Zn–Cu voltaic cell (1.00 M, 25°C) has a standard cell EMF of 1.10 V. On the Reactivity Series / Reduction Potentials pages you can find the two half-cell (standard electrode) potentials: E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76 V and E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V.

Q4. Using E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76 V and E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V, calculate the predicted standard cell EMF.

Q5. Which formula correctly relates E°cell to the two half-cell potentials?
Remember: the cathode is always the electrode with the more positive (or less negative) E° — it is the one being reduced. The anode has the more negative (or less positive) E° — it is the one being oxidised.
Stage 4

The Reactivity Series is a numerical scale

The Reactivity Series isn’t just an order — each metal has an actual number (its standard electrode potential, E°) attached to it. That means you can predict the EMF of any pair of metals before you even build the cell, just by subtracting their two E° values. Try it for these two pairs, then check your prediction against the simulator.
