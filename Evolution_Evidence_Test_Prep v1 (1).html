⚠️ Critical Concept First

Analogous vs Homologous

Before looking at the evidence, you need to understand why the claim mentions flying and being nocturnal — and why those traits DON'T count as evidence.

⚠️ The Trap in This Assessment

The claim specifically mentions that bats and owls both fly and are both nocturnal. This is to test whether you can recognise that these shared traits are NOT evidence of common ancestry — they're analogous structures, which evolved independently in two unrelated lineages.

🔴 Analogous Structures

Structures that look similar and do the same job, but evolved independently in two unrelated lineages. They arose because of similar environmental pressures — NOT because of shared ancestry.

Example: Bat wings and Owl wings both allow flight, but bat wings are modified arm bones while owl wings are modified forelimb feathers. They work similarly but are built differently — and arose separately.

🟢 Homologous Structures

Structures that share the same underlying origin — the same ancestral structure — even if they look different or do different jobs today. These ARE evidence of common ancestry.

Example: The bat wing, human arm, dolphin flipper, and bird wing all contain the SAME bones (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, phalanges) because they all evolved from the same ancestor's forelimb.
💡 Rule of thumb: If two organisms share a trait because they face the same environmental challenge (e.g. both need to fly), it could be analogous. If they share the same underlying structure or genetic sequence, it's likely homologous — and that's evidence of common ancestry.

💭 Think first, then reveal
Why is homologous structure evidence generally useful for determining common ancestry? Think about your answer, then reveal the model response.
★ Intermediate answer
Homologous structures are useful because organisms that share the same bones must have inherited them from a common ancestor. If different animals have the same structures, it suggests they are related.
★★ Proficient answer (adds specifics)
Homologous structures are useful because shared structural features (particularly internal bone patterns) are inherited from a common ancestor — they wouldn't look the same by coincidence. The more similar the bone structure, the more recent the common ancestor, because less evolutionary time has passed for the bones to change.
★★★ Advanced answer (brings in deeper reasoning)
Homologous structures are powerful evidence because the same bone arrangement would not arise independently in unrelated lineages — natural selection would produce different solutions. The shared bones represent an inherited blueprint from a common ancestor. Additionally, the degree of similarity tells us how recent the ancestor is: structures that are more similar suggest more recent divergence, because fewer mutations have modified them since the split.
💭 What SPECIFIC patterns support the claim?
Looking at Evidence A, what specific pattern suggests bats have a more recent common ancestor with humans than with owls/birds? Be as specific as possible.
★★ Proficient — specific reference to evidence
The bat forelimb has four very long digits (phalanges) forming its wing — the same number and type of bones as in the human hand, which also has five long digits. The bird (chicken) wing, by contrast, has highly reduced and fused digits — a very different bone arrangement. This shows the bat and human forelimb are more structurally similar to each other than either is to the bird, suggesting bats and humans share a more recent common ancestor.
★★★ Advanced — adds evolutionary reasoning
Both the bat wing and human arm share elongated humerus, radius, and a full set of five phalanges — structures inherited from their shared mammalian ancestor. The bird wing has fused carpometacarpus bones and reduced/absent digit bones, which are modifications that occurred in the bird lineage long after birds diverged from the mammal lineage. The structural similarity between bat and human forelimbs is therefore homologous (inherited), while any similarity between bat and bird wings is analogous (both evolved for flight separately). This supports the claim that bats share a more recent ancestor with humans.

💭 Think first, then reveal
Why are shared behavioral and physical traits generally useful as evidence for common ancestry? Think carefully — why can't it just be a coincidence?
★ Intermediate
Shared traits suggest common ancestry because organisms that share many of the same characteristics are likely to have inherited them from a shared ancestor.
★★ Proficient
Shared physical and behavioral traits (especially complex ones like live birth and producing milk through mammary glands) are unlikely to have arisen multiple times independently. When two species share these traits while a third doesn't, it suggests the two species diverged from each other more recently than either did from the third species — they still share traits inherited from their more recent common ancestor.
★★★ Advanced
Complex biological traits like mammary glands and live birth require many genetic changes to evolve. It is statistically extremely unlikely that these evolved identically and independently in two unrelated lineages. Their presence in both bats and humans — but absence in owls — indicates a shared inherited origin: they were both present in the common mammalian ancestor of bats and humans, and retained in both lineages after they diverged. The more unique and complex a shared trait, the more strongly it indicates common ancestry.
💭 Specific patterns that support the claim
Look carefully at the table. How many traits do bats share with humans but NOT with owls? Name them. How does this support the claim?
★★ Proficient
Bats share three traits with humans that owls do not share: (1) live birth rather than egg-laying, (2) feeding young through mammary glands rather than regurgitation, and (3) having fur/hair rather than feathers. These three traits are defining mammalian features. Owls, as birds, have none of these. The pattern shows that bats are more closely aligned with humans in their fundamental biological characteristics — supporting the claim that bats and humans share a more recent common ancestor.
★★★ Advanced
Three out of four traits show bats align with humans, not owls: live birth, mammary gland feeding, and fur. These three traits are the core defining features of class Mammalia. Owls, as birds (class Aves), lack all three. Crucially, these are not superficial similarities — mammary glands are a complex organ system that evolved once in the mammalian lineage. The fact that bats and humans both possess this trait while owls (with whom bats share the analogous traits of flying and being nocturnal) do not, directly supports the claim: the similarities between bats and owls are superficial (analogous), while the similarities between bats and humans reflect deep shared ancestry (homologous).

Evidence C

Transitional Fossils

The fossil record — showing intermediate organisms that link one group to another through geological time.

Evidence C — Transitional organisms: Ptilocercus → Icaronycteris
Ptilocercus
A small, shrew-like proto-mammal. Four-legged, no wings. Related to the common ancestor of both humans and bats. Body plan similar to early primates.
→
Hypothetical Transitional Taxon 1
Slightly larger. Forelimb bones beginning to elongate. Small skin membranes visible between arm and body. Still primarily a ground/tree dweller.
→
Hypothetical Transitional Taxon 2
Expanded wing membranes between elongated finger bones. More bat-like posture. Mammalian features (fur, body plan) clearly visible.
→
Icaronycteris
The earliest known bat fossil (~52 million years ago). Fully formed bat wings — highly elongated finger bones with membrane. Clearly mammalian skeleton throughout.

🔑 Key: The entire sequence goes from a mammal (Ptilocercus, related to humans) toward a bat — NOT from a bird toward a bat. The bat body plan evolved from within the mammal lineage.

💭 Why are transitional fossils useful?
Why does finding a sequence of transitional fossils help us determine common ancestry? What would it mean if there were NO transitional forms?
★ Intermediate
Transitional fossils are useful because they show intermediate stages between two different types of organism, demonstrating that one evolved from the other over time.
★★ Proficient
Transitional fossils show organisms that have characteristics of two different groups, demonstrating the evolutionary pathway from one to the other. They provide a physical record of gradual change over time. If we find a sequence of fossils showing step-by-step changes from one body plan toward another, it is strong evidence that these groups share a common ancestor — one evolved from a lineage shared with the other.
★★★ Advanced
Transitional fossils are particularly powerful evidence because they show not just that two groups are related, but how the transition occurred — which traits changed in which order. This rules out the explanation that two groups just happen to look similar by coincidence. The presence of specifically mammalian features (fur, mammalian skull structure, mammalian limb plan) throughout the Ptilocercus-to-Icaronycteris sequence means we can positively identify that bats evolved from within the mammal lineage, ruling out any close relationship with birds at this evolutionary timeframe.
💭 Specific patterns that support the claim
What is the most important thing about Evidence C that directly supports the claim? Hint: think about where the sequence STARTS.
★★ Proficient
The most important feature is that the sequence begins with Ptilocercus — a proto-mammal that is also an ancestor of humans. This means the bat lineage evolved directly from within the mammal group. Bats did not evolve from a bird ancestor — they evolved from the same mammalian line that also gave rise to humans. This directly supports the claim that bats share a more recent common ancestor with humans (the shared mammalian ancestor) than with owls (which evolved separately in the bird lineage).
★★★ Advanced
The sequence starts with Ptilocercus — a shrew-like proto-mammal that is explicitly stated to be "also related to humans." This establishes that the bat lineage branched off from within the mammal family tree, which itself branched away from birds hundreds of millions of years earlier. Throughout the entire sequence from Ptilocercus to Icaronycteris, every organism retains mammalian characteristics (body plan, skull structure). No bird-like features appear at any point. This rules out any close evolutionary relationship between bats and owls — their flight arose via completely different evolutionary pathways in completely different lineages.

This similarity between bat and human feet suggests they inherited the same foot structure from their shared mammalian ancestor, while owls evolved a different foot structure in the bird lineage.

💡 Why does embryo similarity matter? The instructions for building an embryo are written in DNA. If two organisms develop in very similar ways, it means their developmental gene sequences are very similar — inherited from a shared common ancestor. The embryo development program is one of the most conserved (least changed) parts of an organism's genome.
💭 Specific patterns that support the claim
What does the visual similarity between bat and human embryos suggest, and why does this support the claim about common ancestry?
★★ Proficient
The bat and human embryos look strikingly similar at mid-stage — both are rounded, compact, and have similarly proportioned heads and limb buds. The owl embryo looks noticeably different, with a more elongated shape and differently positioned limb buds. This similarity in development suggests that bat and human embryos follow a similar developmental program, which they inherited from their shared mammalian common ancestor. Organisms that develop similarly share similar developmental genes, which are passed down through shared ancestry.
★★★ Advanced
The developmental gene sequences that control embryonic body plan are among the most conserved in all of biology. If bat and human embryos are building themselves in a similar way, it means they are using very similar developmental instructions — almost certainly inherited from a shared common ancestor rather than evolved independently. The fact that the owl embryo looks distinctly different supports the cladistic picture: birds diverged from the mammal lineage hundreds of millions of years ago, giving their developmental program time to diverge. Bats and humans, which are both mammals, diverged much more recently, so their developmental programs are still very similar.

Evidence F

DNA Cladogram

A cladogram built from comparative DNA analysis — the most direct evidence of evolutionary relationships.

Evidence F — Cladogram based on comparative DNA analysis
Humans + Bats node (more recent split)
Birds + Mammals split (more ancient)
Chicken + Owl node (bird ancestor)
💡 How to read a cladogram: Each branching point (node) represents a common ancestor. If two organisms share a node that is higher up on the time axis (closer to the present), they share a more recent common ancestor. Humans and bats share a node that is more recent than the node where the bird lineage and mammal lineage split.
💭 Specific patterns that support the claim
Read the cladogram carefully. Explain what the position of the humans+bats node tells us about the claim. Compare it to where bats and owls would meet on the cladogram.
★★ Proficient
The cladogram shows humans and bats sharing a branching point (node) that is more recent (higher on the time axis, closer to the present) than the point where the bird lineage and the mammal lineage diverge. To trace from a bat to an owl on this cladogram, you would have to go all the way back to the ancient common ancestor of all birds and mammals — much further back in time. But to trace from a bat to a human, you only go back to the more recent mammal common ancestor. This directly shows that bats and humans share a more recent common ancestor than bats and owls.
★★★ Advanced
This cladogram is built from comparative DNA analysis, which makes it particularly reliable — DNA similarity directly reflects evolutionary distance. The humans+bats node is positioned more recently on the time axis than the root node (where birds and mammals split). To find a common ancestor shared by a bat and an owl, you must travel back to the root — the point at which the entire bird and mammal lineages diverged, which occurred hundreds of millions of years ago. The fact that this cladogram is based on DNA means it captures the cumulative mutation differences between species: humans and bats share far more of their DNA than either does with owls, because they have had less time to accumulate genetic differences since their more recent common ancestor.

Your Test

The CER Framework

The test asks for Claim → Evidence → Reasoning. Understanding the rubric levels will help you know what to write.

★ Intermediate — The starting point
  • Correctly names the two types of evidence you chose (e.g., "comparative anatomy" or "transitional fossils")
  • Explains IN GENERAL TERMS why that type of evidence is useful for determining common ancestry — without referencing the specific evidence in front of you
★★ Proficient — The target
  • Everything in Intermediate PLUS:
  • References SPECIFIC DETAILS from the evidence in front of you (e.g., "In Evidence B, bats share live birth, mammary glands, and fur with humans, but NOT with owls")
  • Links those specific details back to the claim with clear reasoning
★★★ Advanced — Going further
  • Everything in Proficient PLUS:
  • Brings in deeper scientific understanding (e.g., explaining WHY shared traits are unlikely to be coincidence, or discussing analogous vs homologous)
  • Makes connections between different pieces of evidence
  • Uses precise scientific vocabulary accurately throughout

Your two-part response structure

For each piece of evidence you choose, you write TWO things:

Box 1 — General usefulness: "This type of evidence is useful in general for determining common ancestry because [reason why this type of evidence works as a principle]..."
Box 2 — Specific patterns: "In this particular evidence, the pattern that supports the claim is [specific observation]. This supports the claim because [reasoning that links the observation to the conclusion about bats, humans, and owls]..."
💡 Choosing your evidence: You need two pieces of different types. The strongest choices for this claim tend to be Evidence B (traits), Evidence F (cladogram), Evidence A (homologous structures), or Evidence C (transitional fossils) — but any two will work if your reasoning is sound.
