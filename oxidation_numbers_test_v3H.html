📜 Quick Reference — Rules, Redox & Historical Timeline

Rules for Oxidation Numbers

  • 1. Pure element → 0
  • 2. Monatomic ion → its charge
  • 3. F always → −1
  • 4. O usually → −2 (−1 in peroxides)
  • 5. H with non-metal → +1
  • 5b. H in metal hydrides → −1
  • 6. Group 1 metals → +1
  • 7. Group 2 metals → +2
  • 8. Σ ONs = 0 (neutral) or ion charge

Section 1
Lavoisier & the Birth of Oxidation Theory
France · 1772–1789 · Paris
🇫🇷
Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier — Paris, France, 1772–1789
France under Louis XVI was a nation on the brink — the Ancien Régime creaking toward revolution. Lavoisier, a tax farmer and nobleman, worked in his private laboratory in Paris, performing the most precise quantitative experiments chemistry had yet seen. He named oxygen (1778), demolished the phlogiston theory, and published his landmark Traité Élémentaire de Chimie in 1789 — the same year the Revolution began. Lavoisier was arrested and guillotined on 8 May 1794. The mathematician Lagrange observed: "It took but a moment to cut off that head; a hundred years may not produce another like it."

Section 2
Davy & Faraday — The Age of Electrolysis
England · 1807–1834 · Royal Institution, London
🇬🇧
Sir Humphry Davy & Michael Faraday — London, England
Britain in 1807 was fighting Napoleon — her coastline blockaded, her economy strained, yet her scientific institutions flourishing. Sir Humphry Davy at the Royal Institution in Albemarle Street, London, used a powerful voltaic pile to pass electricity through molten metal hydroxides. In a single week in October 1807 he isolated potassium, then sodium — the first elements ever isolated by electricity. He watched potassium burst into purple flame on water and wept with joy. His young bookbinder's apprentice assistant, Michael Faraday (born Newington Butts, Surrey, 1791), would go on to surpass him — formulating the laws of electrolysis in 1833–34 and transforming our understanding of how electricity drives chemical change.
💡 Electrolysis reminder At the cathode (negative electrode): cations gain electrons → reduction (ON decreases).
At the anode (positive electrode): anions lose electrons → oxidation (ON increases).

Section 3
Bessemer & the Contact Process — Industrial Revolution
England · 1831 & 1856 · Bristol & Sheffield
🇬🇧
Peregrine Phillips (Bristol, 1831) & Henry Bessemer (Sheffield, 1856)
Victorian Britain was the workshop of the world — its mills, railways, and shipyards demanding ever cheaper steel and acid in industrial quantities. Peregrine Phillips, a Bristol vinegar merchant with no university education, quietly patented the Contact Process in 1831: a method to oxidise sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide over a platinum catalyst, the basis of modern sulfuric acid production. Twenty-five years later, Henry Bessemer unveiled his revolutionary converter in Sheffield — a pear-shaped vessel that blew air through molten pig iron at ferocious speed, oxidising out carbon, silicon, and manganese impurities and producing cheap steel within minutes. Before Bessemer, steel was a luxury; after him, it built the modern world.

Section 4
Haber-Bosch & Ostwald — Germany's Chemical Revolution
Germany · 1902–1913 · Karlsruhe, Leipzig, Ludwigshafen
🇩🇪
Fritz Haber, Carl Bosch & Wilhelm Ostwald — Imperial Germany
Kaiser Wilhelm II's Germany was Europe's industrial and chemical superpower. By 1900 Germany produced more steel than Britain. Wilhelm Ostwald (Leipzig, 1902) — Nobel Prize 1909 — developed the catalytic oxidation of ammonia to produce nitric acid, vital for both fertilisers and explosives. Then in 1909, Fritz Haber (Karlsruhe) demonstrated the synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen under high pressure. Carl Bosch at BASF (Ludwigshafen) scaled this to industrial production by 1913. The Haber-Bosch Process is arguably the most consequential chemical discovery in history: it feeds roughly half the world's population today. Yet Haber's legacy is deeply ambiguous — he also directed Germany's chemical weapons programme in WWI, deploying chlorine gas at Ypres, Belgium, in April 1915.

Section 5
Hall-Héroult & Thermite — Redox in Metals
USA, France & Germany · 1886–1893
🇺🇸🇫🇷🇩🇪
Hall (Ohio, USA), Héroult (France) & Goldschmidt (Essen, Germany)
One of the most remarkable coincidences in all of science: Charles Martin Hall in his woodshed in Oberlin, Ohio, and Paul Héroult in Gentilly near Paris independently discovered the same electrolytic process for aluminium on the same year — 1886. Both were 22 years old; both were born in 1863; both died in 1914. Before their discovery, aluminium was literally more precious than gold — Napoleon III of France served aluminium cutlery to his most distinguished guests while lesser guests used gold. The Hall-Héroult Process made aluminium cheap enough to build aeroplanes. Meanwhile in Essen, Germany (Kaiserreich Prussia), Hans Goldschmidt developed the thermite reaction in 1893 — burning aluminium with iron oxide to generate molten iron at 2500 °C, which welders used to fuse together the Kaiserreich's rapidly expanding railway network.
