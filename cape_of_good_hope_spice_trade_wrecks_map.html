Wrecks of the Spice Route

Cape of Good Hope · VOC & East India Company ships, 1647–1778

Place names in blue link to Wikipedia · schematic map, not to nautical scale.
1 · Nieuwe Haerlem — VOC, 25 Mar 1647, Milnerton Beach. Ran aground homeward bound; survivors' report persuaded the VOC to found a resupply station at the Cape in 1652.
2 · Goede Hoop & Orange — VOC & English EIC, 5 Jun 1692, Salt River mouth. A Dutch pinnace and an English East Indiaman lost in the same gale.
3 · Oosterland & Waddinxveen — VOC, 24 May 1697, Salt River mouth. The Oosterland carried Huguenot refugee families to the Cape; both wrecked in one storm.
4 · Het Huis te Craijestein — VOC, 27 May 1698, Oudekraal. Wrecked on its maiden voyage when its anchor cable parted in fog; cannon and timbers remain among the boulders.
5 · 1722 storm fleet — VOC & EIC, 16 Jun 1722, Table Bay. At least eight East Indiamen (Addison, Chandois, Nightingale, Gouda, Lakenman, Rotterdam, Standvastigheid, Zoetigheid) destroyed in a single gale.
6 · Reigersdaal — VOC, 25 Oct 1747, Silwerstroomstrand. Wrecked in shallow water north of Table Bay; anchors and cannon still visible.
7 · 1737 storm fleet — VOC, 21 May 1737, Table Bay. The worst disaster in VOC history: eight ships and 208 men lost in one night, nearly 30% of that year's cargo value.
8 · Jonge Thomas — VOC, 1 Jun 1773, Salt River mouth. Farmer Wolraad Woltemade rescued 14 sailors on horseback before he and his horse drowned.
9 · Nieuwe Rhoon — VOC, 31 Jan 1776, Whale Rock. Struck the rock in a gale; wreckage rediscovered beneath the Cape Town Civic Centre foundations in 1970.
10 · Colebrooke — British EIC, 25 Aug 1778, Kogel Bay, False Bay. Deliberately grounded after striking an uncharted rock; copper and lead cargo recovered in 1984.
Most individual wrecks link to their entry on Wikipedia — Shipwrecks of Cape Town, the source for this map.
