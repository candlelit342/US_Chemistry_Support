Selected works: In Memoriam A.H.H. (1850) · Idylls of the King (1859) · Crossing the Bar (1889)

Alfred Lord Tennyson was a British poet who served as Poet Laureate for over forty years and is one of the most quoted poets in the English language. In Memoriam A.H.H. (1850) and Idylls of the King (1859–85) are his greatest achievements.

Most recent: Crossing the Bar (1889)

Andrew Lang Wikipedia ↗

b. 1844 📍 Selkirk, Scotland 🎓 University of St Andrews; Balliol College, Oxford
Selected works: Custom and Myth (1884) · The Blue Fairy Book (1889) · Myth, Ritual and Religion (1887)

Andrew Lang was a Scottish poet, novelist, and literary critic known above all for the Fairy Books series, twelve volumes of fairy tales beginning with The Blue Fairy Book (1889). He was also an important early anthropologist.

Most recent: The Blue Fairy Book (1889)

Leo Tolstoy Wikipedia ↗

b. 1828 📍 Yasnaya Polyana, Tula Oblast, Russia 🎓 Kazan Imperial University (left)
Selected works: War and Peace (1869) · Anna Karenina (1877) · The Death of Ivan Ilyich (1886)

Leo Tolstoy was a Russian novelist widely regarded as the greatest novelist who ever lived. War and Peace (1869) and Anna Karenina (1877) are cornerstones of world literature, combining vast narrative scope with profound moral and psychological insight.

Most recent: The Death of Ivan Ilyich (1886)

Edward Lear Wikipedia ↗

b. 1812 📍 Holloway, London, England 🎓 Did not attend university
Selected works: A Book of Nonsense (1846) · Nonsense Songs (1871) · Laughable Lyrics (1877)

Edward Lear was a British artist, illustrator, musician, and poet known for his nonsense verse, especially A Book of Nonsense (1846) and limericks. He also painted landscapes of the Mediterranean and the Near East.

Most recent: Laughable Lyrics (1877)

Ivan Turgenev Wikipedia ↗

b. 1818 📍 Oryol, Russia 🎓 University of Moscow; Saint Petersburg; University of Berlin
Selected works: Rudin (1856) · Fathers and Sons (1862) · Virgin Soil (1877)

Ivan Turgenev was a Russian novelist and short story writer known for Fathers and Sons (1862), which gave the world the concept of nihilism. He was the first Russian author to achieve major fame in Western Europe.

Most recent: Virgin Soil (1877)

Alexander Ostrovsky Wikipedia ↗

b. 1823 📍 Moscow, Russia 🎓 Moscow State University (Law)
Selected works: The Storm (1860) · Poverty Is No Vice (1854) · The Forest (1871)

Alexander Ostrovsky was a Russian playwright widely regarded as the father of the Russian theatre tradition. His plays, including The Storm (1860), depicted merchant-class Moscow with great psychological and social acuity.

Most recent: The Forest (1871)

Alexander Herzen Wikipedia ↗

b. 1812 📍 Moscow, Russia 🎓 Imperial Moscow University
Selected works: Who Is to Blame? (1847) · From the Other Shore (1850) · My Past and Thoughts (1867)

Alexander Herzen was a Russian writer and political thinker known as the father of Russian socialism. My Past and Thoughts (1852–68), his multi-volume memoir, is one of the greatest autobiographies in world literature.

Most recent: My Past and Thoughts (1867)

William Wordsworth Wikipedia ↗

b. 1770 📍 Cockermouth, Cumberland, England 🎓 St John's College, Cambridge
Selected works: Lyrical Ballads (1798) · The Prelude (1850) · The Excursion (1814)

William Wordsworth was an English Romantic poet who, along with Samuel Taylor Coleridge, launched the Romantic Age in English literature with their collaborative Lyrical Ballads (1798). The Prelude, his autobiographical poem, is one of the great poems in the language.

Most recent: The Prelude (1850)

Jane Austen Wikipedia ↗

b. 1775 📍 Steventon, Hampshire, England 🎓 Did not attend university
Selected works: Sense and Sensibility (1811) · Pride and Prejudice (1813) · Emma (1815)

Jane Austen was an English novelist whose six major works, including Pride and Prejudice (1813) and Emma (1815), have earned her a place among the greatest writers in the English language. Her ironic social observation and complex heroines have made her endlessly influential.

Most recent: Emma (1815)

Alexander Pope Wikipedia ↗

b. 1688 📍 London, England 🎓 Self-educated
Selected works: The Rape of the Lock (1714) · An Essay on Man (1733) · The Dunciad (1743)

Alexander Pope was an English poet of the Augustan period known for the satirical poem The Rape of the Lock (1712) and his verse translations of Homer. He was the leading poet of his era and is the third-most-quoted writer in the English language.

Most recent: The Dunciad (1743)

Jonathan Swift Wikipedia ↗

b. 1667 📍 Dublin, Ireland 🎓 Trinity College Dublin; Hart Hall, Oxford (MA)
Selected works: A Tale of a Tub (1704) · Gulliver's Travels (1726) · A Modest Proposal (1729)

Jonathan Swift was an Anglo-Irish satirist whose Gulliver's Travels (1726) and A Modest Proposal (1729) are masterpieces of satirical literature. He was Dean of St Patrick's Cathedral in Dublin and a fierce advocate for Irish rights.

Most recent: A Modest Proposal (1729)

Ben Jonson Wikipedia ↗

b. 1572 📍 Westminster, London, England 🎓 Did not attend university
Selected works: Volpone (1606) · The Alchemist (1610) · Bartholomew Fair (1614)

Ben Jonson was an English playwright and poet who was the dominant figure in Jacobean theatre alongside Shakespeare. His comedies Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are sharp satirical exposés of human greed and folly.

Most recent: Bartholomew Fair (1614)

Sophocles Wikipedia ↗

b. c. 497 BC 📍 Colonus, near Athens, Greece 🎓 Educated under Hellenic system by Lamprus
Selected works: Antigone (441 BC) · Oedipus Rex (429 BC) · Oedipus at Colonus (401 BC)

Sophocles was an ancient Greek playwright who wrote over one hundred and twenty plays, of which seven survive complete. Oedipus the King and Antigone are among the most performed and studied plays in the history of theatre.

Most recent: Oedipus at Colonus (401 BC)

Euripides Wikipedia ↗

b. c. 480 BC 📍 Salamis (or Phlya, Attica), Greece 🎓 Educated by sophists (pre-university)
Selected works: Medea (431 BC) · The Bacchae (405 BC) · Electra (420 BC)

Euripides was an ancient Greek playwright who brought a new psychological realism and sympathy for women and slaves to Greek tragedy. Medea and The Bacchae are among his most celebrated and frequently performed works.

Most recent: The Bacchae (405 BC)
