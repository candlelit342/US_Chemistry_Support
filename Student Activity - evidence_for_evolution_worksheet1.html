Station 1 · Fossils

Evidence Type #1

🔑 Key Terms

Fossil — preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms
Transition fossil — a fossil of a species that existed between an ancient and a modern form, showing gradual change

Fossils are the most obvious form of evidence for evolution. Without them, we would have virtually no clues about the species that existed in the past. On his journey, Darwin found fossils of ancient forms of modern species. From their similarities he determined their relationships, but their differences convinced him that species have changed.


Technically, the discovery of a single fossil doesn't prove that an ancient species is related to a modern one, no matter how similar they are. If scientists can find a set of fossils from various time periods that show gradual changes, their argument is much more convincing. Scientists search for transition fossils — fossils of species that existed between an ancient and modern form — in order to strengthen the case for evolution.

Station 2 · Anatomy

Evidence Type #2

🔑 Key Terms

Anatomy — the study of body structure
Homologous structures — similar structures in different species that suggest a common ancestor
Analogous structures — structures with the same function but NOT inherited from a common ancestor

Scientists examine anatomy, or body structure, to determine relationships between species. Body structure is determined by DNA, so species with similar anatomies also have similar DNA — and individuals with more similar DNA are more closely related.


Homologous structures are structures that are similar across species even though they may be used for different purposes (walking, flying, swimming, etc.). For example, a human arm, a horse leg, a cat paw, a bat wing, a bird wing, and a whale flipper all share the same basic bone structure: one solid upper arm bone, two forearm bones, and several small "finger" bones. Scientists believe they all share a common ancestor that had this same arrangement.


Analogous structures are a common trap! Just because two species look alike doesn't mean they're related. Analogous structures do the same job and may look similar, but were NOT inherited from the same ancestor. For example, bird wings and insect wings are both used for flying — but they are so different in structure that scientists do NOT consider them evidence of a shared ancestor.

Station 3 · Vestigial Structures

Evidence Type #3

🔑 Key Term

Vestigial structure — a body part that appears to have no current purpose but likely had a function in the past

Vestigial structures are parts of the body that seem to have no purpose at all. Scientists suspect that vestigial structures are evidence that these parts did serve a purpose in the past.


For example, whales have a pelvis (hip) bone near where their hind limbs would be. It is thought that this bone provides evidence that the whale evolved from a species which had hind limbs and may have even walked on land. Perhaps after this species moved into the water, it no longer needed hind legs. These structures gradually shrank over generations until they almost disappeared — but not completely. Today the whale's vestigial pelvis remains to tell their story.

Station 4 · Embryology

Evidence Type #4

🔑 Key Term

Embryo — an organism in its earliest stage of development, before birth or hatching

Another way scientists use anatomy as evidence is by comparing embryos — organisms in their earliest stage of development. DNA determines most of an organism's body structure. Therefore, more similar anatomy indicates similar DNA, which suggests close relationships.


Sometimes the embryos of two species look very similar even when their adult forms are quite different. For example, at an early stage of development, the embryos of a rabbit, chicken, human, cow, salamander, and fish all look remarkably alike. Scientists believe these similarities provide evidence that these very different species share a common ancestor far back in their evolutionary history.

Station 5 · DNA Comparison

Evidence Type #5

🔑 Key Terms

DNA sequencing — reading the order of genetic "letters" (nucleotides: A, T, C, G) inside a cell
Nucleotide — a single genetic "letter" that makes up DNA

Modern technology allows us to look inside the cells of organisms and sequence their DNA. The more nucleotides, or genetic "letters," shared by two individuals, the more closely they are thought to be related.

Station 6 · Direct Evidence

Evidence Type #6

🔑 Key Term

Microevolution — small changes in a species over a short period of time, observable directly
Natural selection — the process by which individuals with more favorable traits survive and pass their genes on

Finally, we are able to directly observe microevolution, or small changes in a species over a short period of time! As new dog breeds, more drought-resistant crops, and more infectious viruses continue to develop, scientists have no choice but to accept that change within a species is possible.


As environments change, natural selection continues to work: it eliminates individuals with less favorable traits and allows the "fittest" to survive and pass on their genes to the next generation. Over many generations, this process leads to gradual change — evolution in action!
