► The Crack — A Note for the Curious Student

Big Ben rang for the first time on 11 July 1859. Within two months it had cracked. The cause: the hammer supplied by the foundry weighed 660 lb (300 kg) — far heavier than specification. Repeated blows split the bronze along a fissure roughly 1.2 metres long. Rather than recast, the bell was turned 90 degrees so the clapper would strike fresh metal, and a lighter hammer fitted. Big Ben has rung from that turned position ever since.

What the crack does acoustically. The crack destroys the bell's rotational symmetry, forcing each vibrational mode to split into two slightly different frequencies (doublet), one for each flexing plane. In an undamaged bell the two doublet components are nearly identical; in Big Ben the crack widens every doublet. The amplitude difference between in-phase and out-of-phase states creates the famous warble at 1.5 Hz on the Nominal.

Why it sounds like A, not E. Despite a Nominal of ~335 Hz (E4), most listeners perceive Big Ben as A4 (440 Hz). A strong partial cluster near 883 Hz triggers a virtual pitch percept — the brain infers a missing fundamental of 440 Hz.

► True-Harmonic Tuning — A Note for Students

The distinction: the doublet beating modelled here (the pulse within each partial) comes from physical asymmetry in the casting: uneven wall thickness causes each vibrational mode to split. No tuning process eliminates it; only a more perfectly symmetric casting would narrow it.

What the true-harmonic (Taylor/Simpson) system addressed was a different roughness. In old-style English bells the Hum partial sat about 2 semitones sharp and the Prime about 1.5 semitones flat of where they would need to be to form clean octaves with the Nominal. Canon Arthur Simpson described this in 1895–96 papers, and Taylor's achieved consistent true-harmonic tuning by 1896: Hum = 2 octaves below Nominal · Prime = 1 octave below Nominal. The bell is lathe-turned inside to bring all five partials to exact harmonic ratios.

Continental traditions: German founders worked in the Nominalton tradition — tuning the Nominal precisely while allowing other partials to sit where the profile places them. Both traditions are beautiful; they sound differently.
