✅ Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

  • Rule 1: Pure element = 0
    Fe, O₂, N₂, Al, Cu = 0
  • Rule 2: Monatomic ion = its charge
    Na⁺=+1, Fe³⁺=+3, Cl⁻=-1
  • Rule 3: F always = −1 in compounds
  • Rule 4: O usually = −2
    Exception: −1 in peroxides (H₂O₂)
  • Rule 5: H = +1 (with non-metals)
    H = −1 in metal hydrides (NaH)
  • Rule 6: Group 1 metals = +1 always
  • Rule 7: Group 2 metals = +2 always
  • Rule 8: Sum of all ONs = 0 (neutral molecule) or = ion charge

🔢 Step-by-Step Method — Worked Example

Find the ON of N in KNO₃:

  • Step 1 Write what you know:
    K = +1  |  O = −2 (×3 = −6)
  • Step 2 Molecule is neutral → sum = 0
  • Step 3 (+1) + N + (−6) = 0
  • Step 4 N = 0 − 1 + 6 = +5 ✓
  • Try SO₄²⁻ for S:
    O = −2 (×4 = −8), ion charge = −2
    S + (−8) = −2 → S = +6
  • Try NH₄⁺ for N:
    H = +1 (×4 = +4), ion charge = +1
    N + 4 = +1 → N = −3

⚡ OIL RIG — The Golden Rule

OIL
Oxidation Is Loss
of electrons
→ ON increases
→ the species is oxidised
→ it is the reducing agent
RIG
Reduction Is Gain
of electrons
→ ON decreases
→ the species is reduced
→ it is the oxidising agent

⚠️ The reducing agent is itself oxidised.
The oxidising agent is itself reduced.
Oxidation and reduction always happen together.

Section 1
Ancient Redox — Gunpowder & Iron Smelting
China, c.850 AD & Middle East, c.1200 BC
🧨⚔️
China, c.850 AD · Middle East & Europe, c.1200 BC
Tang Dynasty China: alchemists searching for immortality mixed charcoal, sulfur, and potassium nitrate (saltpetre). The result killed several of them and burned down a laboratory. They had discovered gunpowder — the most consequential chemical reaction in human history, which would topple castle walls, end feudal warfare, and redraw the map of every civilisation. The key chemistry: potassium nitrate provides oxygen that violently oxidises the carbon, releasing energy in milliseconds.
Three thousand years earlier, peoples of the Middle East discovered that heating certain rocks (iron ores) with charcoal produced a hard, workable metal. This was iron smelting — the first large-scale industrial reduction reaction. It ended the Bronze Age, gave ordinary soldiers cheap weapons, and launched the Iron Age. The blast furnace was its industrial successor, burning coal-derived coke to produce carbon monoxide, which reduced iron ore to metallic iron at scale.

Section 2
The Oxygen Revolution — Lavoisier & Photosynthesis
Paris, France, 1772–1789 & Earth, 3.5 billion years ago
🇫🇷🌿
Lavoisier (Paris, 1772–1789) & Photosynthesis (Earth, c.3.5 billion years ago)
Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier worked in pre-Revolutionary Paris under Louis XVI — a society on the edge of catastrophe. In his private laboratory he performed the most precise chemical experiments the world had seen, weighing sealed vessels before and after combustion to prove that matter is conserved and that burning requires oxygen. He named oxygen in 1778 and demolished the phlogiston theory. He was arrested after the Revolution and guillotined on 8 May 1794, aged 50. The mathematician Lagrange said: "It took but a moment to cut off that head; a hundred years may not produce another like it."

Photosynthesis is an older story — a reaction that began roughly 3.5 billion years ago when cyanobacteria first evolved the ability to use sunlight to reduce CO₂ into organic carbon, releasing O₂ as a byproduct. Around 2.4 billion years ago this caused the Great Oxidation Event, flooding Earth's atmosphere with oxygen and wiping out most anaerobic life. Every breath you take is a product of that ancient redox reaction.
💡 Reminder: comparing photosynthesis and respiration They are exact redox opposites. In photosynthesis: C is reduced (+4→0) and O is oxidised (−2→0 in O₂). In respiration: C is oxidised (0→+4) and O is reduced (0→−2). Same atoms, reversed electron flow.

Section 3
The Age of Electricity — Davy's Electrolysis
Royal Institution, London, England · 1807–1808
🇬🇧⚡
Sir Humphry Davy — Royal Institution, Albemarle Street, London, 1807
Britain in 1807 was fighting Napoleon — her ports blockaded, her economy under strain, yet her scientific societies flourishing. Sir Humphry Davy, 28 years old, used a powerful voltaic pile (an early battery) to pass electricity through molten potassium hydroxide. He watched a small metal appear at the cathode and burst into brilliant purple flame on contact with water. He had isolated potassium — an element that had never previously existed in pure form anywhere on Earth. Days later he repeated the experiment with sodium. He was so excited he reportedly danced around the laboratory. The following year he isolated calcium, magnesium, strontium, and barium — essentially creating an entire column of the periodic table by electrolysis. His young assistant Michael Faraday, who began as a bookbinder's apprentice, would go on to formulate the laws of electrolysis in 1833–34 from the same building.
💡 Electrolysis reminder — cathode vs anode Cathode (negative): cations are attracted here, GAIN electrons → reduction (ON decreases).
Anode (positive): anions are attracted here, LOSE electrons → oxidation (ON increases).

Section 4
The Industrial Chemical Age — Thermite & Haber-Bosch
Germany · Essen 1893 & Karlsruhe / Ludwigshafen 1909–1913
🇩🇪🔥
Goldschmidt (Essen, 1893) & Haber-Bosch (Karlsruhe / Ludwigshafen, 1909–1913)
Kaiser Wilhelm II's Germany was Europe's industrial and chemical superpower, its steel mills and chemical plants transforming the landscape. In Essen in 1893, Hans Goldschmidt discovered that burning aluminium powder with iron oxide produced molten iron at 2500°C in seconds — enough heat to fuse railway tracks together on site without a furnace. Thermite was born. It welded the Kaiserreich's rapidly expanding railway network, and was later used to destroy the railways of enemies.

In Karlsruhe in 1909, Fritz Haber demonstrated the synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen. Carl Bosch at BASF in Ludwigshafen scaled it to industrial production by 1913. The Haber-Bosch Process is arguably the most consequential chemistry ever devised: today it produces fertiliser that feeds roughly half the world's population. Yet Haber is deeply ambiguous — he directed Germany's chemical weapons programme and deployed chlorine gas at Ypres, Belgium in April 1915. His wife Clara, a chemist, shot herself in their garden the night after his first successful gas attack.

Section 5
The Modern World — Hall-Héroult & Cellular Respiration
Ohio, USA & France · 1886  |  Inside every living cell · always
🇺🇸🇫🇷🫀
Hall & Héroult (1886) · Your Mitochondria (right now)
One of the most astonishing coincidences in science: Charles Martin Hall, 22, working in a woodshed in Oberlin, Ohio, USA, and Paul Héroult, also 22, working in Gentilly near Paris, France, independently discovered the same electrolytic process for producing aluminium in the same year — 1886. Both were born in 1863; both died in 1914. Before their discovery, aluminium was more expensive than gold — Napoleon III served it to his most honoured guests while lesser guests used gold. The Hall-Héroult Process made aluminium cheap enough to build aeroplanes, bicycles, drinks cans, and spacecraft.

Meanwhile, in every cell of your body right now, cellular respiration is oxidising glucose molecules using oxygen, releasing the energy stored by photosynthesis billions of years ago. You are, at this moment, running the same redox chemistry that Lavoisier identified in a sealed flask in Paris in 1772. Evolution has had 600 million years to make it extraordinarily efficient — and it still comes down to electrons moving from glucose to oxygen.
