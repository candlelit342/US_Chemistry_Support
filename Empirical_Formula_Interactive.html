Finding an Empirical Formula from % Composition by Mass

Percentage composition → moles → simplest ratio → empirical formula. Atomic masses from the Cambridge Periodic Table (integers, Cl = 35.5).

1. The Big Idea

An empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound. If you know what percentage of the compound's mass comes from each element, you can work backwards to that ratio.

2. Worked Example — a simple binary compound

A compound is found to contain 60.0% magnesium and 40.0% oxygen by mass. Find its empirical formula. (Ar: Mg = 24, O = 16)

Step 1 — Assume 100 g of compound.
Mass of Mg = 60.0 g    Mass of O = 40.0 g
Step 2 — Convert mass to moles (moles = mass ÷ Ar).
Moles of Mg = 60.0 ÷ 24 = 2.5    Moles of O = 40.0 ÷ 16 = 2.5
Step 3 — Divide every value by the smallest number of moles.
Mg: 2.5 ÷ 2.5 = 1    O: 2.5 ÷ 2.5 = 1
Step 4 — Ratio is already whole numbers → write the empirical formula.
Mg : O = 1 : 1   →   MgO
Magnesium (Mg)Oxygen (O)
% by mass60.040.0
Ar2416
Moles (% ÷ Ar)2.52.5
÷ smallest11

Note: if the ratio isn't a whole number (e.g. 1 : 1.5), multiply every value by the same small number (2, 3, or 4) until they all become whole numbers — you'll practise this below.

The Method (memorise this)

  • Assume you have 100 g of the compound → % becomes grams.
  • Moles = mass ÷ Ar for each element.
  • Divide all mole values by the smallest one.
  • If not whole numbers, multiply all by the same factor:
    • .5 → ×2
    • .33 or .67 → ×3
    • .25 or .75 → ×4
  • Write symbols with these whole numbers as subscripts.

Common mistakes

  • Dividing by Ar the wrong way round (it's mass ÷ Ar, not Ar ÷ mass).
  • Forgetting Cl has Ar = 35.5, not 36 or 35.
  • Rounding too early — keep 2–3 decimal places until the final ratio.
  • Stopping at a decimal ratio instead of scaling up to whole numbers.
