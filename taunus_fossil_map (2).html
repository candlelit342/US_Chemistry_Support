About this Map

This map shows important fossil sites within 60 km of Hohemark, Oberursel (Taunus). Click any marker to see full details: organism, geological age, discovery year and discoverer, and rock type information for students.

Geological Timeline

Devonian Period
419 – 359 million years ago
Taunus Quartzite · Reef Limestone · Phyllite · Greywacke
The Taunus itself is made of Devonian rocks. A warm tropical sea covered the region, producing coral reefs and marine organisms.
Eocene Epoch
56 – 33.9 million years ago
Oil Shale (lacustrine) · Volcanic maar sediment
A volcanic maar lake near Messel trapped and preserved thousands of organisms in fine-grained oil shale. Exceptional soft-tissue preservation.
Oligocene Epoch
33.9 – 23 million years ago
Marine limestone · Sandy marl · Coastal sediments
A shallow subtropical sea covered the Mainz Basin, depositing rich marine sediments with molluscs, sharks, and even whales.
Miocene Epoch
23 – 5.3 million years ago
Fluvial sands · Gravels (Dinotheriensande)
The Proto-Rhine River deposited sandy sediments in Rheinhessen, preserving giant elephant-like mammals under subtropical conditions.

Rock Types — Student Key

Taunus Quartzite (Taunusquarzit)
Very hard metamorphic sandstone, Early Devonian (~400–415 Ma). Originally coastal beach sand, now forms the high Taunus ridgeline. Few fossils due to intense metamorphism.
Reef Limestone (Lahnmarmor)
Middle–Upper Devonian carbonate, ~375–385 Ma. Formed from coral reefs in a tropical shallow sea. Polishes to marble; used in the Empire State Building!
Oil Shale (Ölschiefer)
Middle Eocene lacustrine sediment, ~47–48 Ma. Fine dark laminated mud from the floor of a volcanic maar lake. Exceptional fossil preservation — sometimes with fur and stomach contents!
Dinotheriensande (Fluvial Sands)
Late Miocene fluvial deposits, ~9.5 Ma. Sandy and gravelly river sediments of the ancient Proto-Rhine. Preserves large mammal bones but not soft tissue.
Marine Limestone & Marl (Mainz Basin)
Oligocene marine sediments, ~25–35 Ma. Deposited in a warm shallow sea that covered the Mainz Basin. Contains marine shells, shark teeth, and whale remains.
💡 Student Tip: Why does rock type matter? Fine-grained, oxygen-poor sediments (like oil shale) preserve fossils best — even fur and feathers. Coarse sediments (sands/gravels) preserve bones but lose detail. Metamorphic rocks (quartzite, phyllite) rarely preserve fossils because heat and pressure destroy them.
