Question 2 4 marks — 2 per reason
Why must air be dried before the sulfur burner? Give two reasons.
✓✓
Reason 1 — Catalyst protection: Water vapour poisons the V₂O₅ catalyst, reducing its activity and lifetime. Drying must achieve a dew point of around −40 °C or below.
✓✓
Reason 2 — Acid mist prevention: SO₃ reacts with water vapour in the gas phase: SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄. The product forms as a fine aerosol mist of submicron droplets which passes straight through the absorber towers and exits the stack as a polluting acid plume.
Question 3 4 marks
Why is SO₃ not absorbed directly in water? What is done instead?
✓✓
Problem: SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ is extremely exothermic. The heat vaporises the water and the sulphuric acid forms as a dense submicron aerosol mist — droplets too small to be collected in the absorber towers. The mist escapes as a visible corrosive plume.
✓✓
What is done instead: SO₃ is absorbed into 98% sulphuric acid. In the oleum tower, excess SO₃ forms oleum (H₂S₂O₇). This is blended back with weaker acid or water under controlled conditions to give 98% H₂SO₄. Using exactly 98% acid (not 100%) is critical — 100% acid also causes misting. The oleum / absorber tower combination achieves very high SO₃ absorption efficiency.
Question 4 4 marks — 2 per benefit
Two economic benefits of the waste heat boiler and gas-gas heat exchanger.
✓✓
Waste heat boiler — steam generation: The ~1000 °C combustion gas contains large amounts of thermal energy. Recovering this as high-pressure steam generates a valuable co-product (used on-site or sold to the grid), turning what would otherwise be a waste heat loss into revenue.
✓✓
Gas-gas HX — eliminates external heating cost: Pre-heating the converter feed gas to ~430 °C using the hot exit gas means no external fuel is burned for this purpose. The process is partly “self-heating” — a key factor in making the Contact Process economically competitive compared to earlier chamber processes.

Question 6 — Le Chatelier: Temperature 4 marks
(a) Correct MCQ. (b) Explanation using Le Chatelier’s Principle.
✓
(a) Correct answer: B — “The equilibrium shifts left; less SO₃ is produced at equilibrium.”
✓✓✓
(b) The forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH = −197 kJ mol−1), meaning it releases heat. ✓   By Le Chatelier’s Principle, increasing temperature favours the endothermic (reverse) direction to oppose the change. ✓   The equilibrium shifts left, reducing the proportion of SO₃ and increasing SO₂ and O₂; Kp decreases as temperature increases. ✓
Common misconception: Students often state “higher temperature increases the catalyst activity, giving more product.” The catalyst only affects rate, not the equilibrium position or Kp. Equilibrium yield is governed by ΔH and temperature alone.
Question 7 — Le Chatelier: Pressure 6 marks
(a) Δn. (b) Effect of higher pressure on equilibrium. (c) Two reasons for operating near atmospheric pressure.
✓
(a) Reactant side: 3 mol gas (2 SO₂ + 1 O₂). Product side: 2 mol gas (2 SO₃). Δn = 2 − 3 = −1
✓✓
(b) Increasing pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gas (right side, Δn = −1), so the equilibrium shifts right, increasing SO₃ yield. ✓   The simulation confirms: at 450 °C, α rises from 71.5% at 0.5 atm to 88.1% at 5 atm. ✓
✓✓
(c) Reasons for low pressure (any two):
  • At 1–2 atm, multi-pass operation already achieves ~97% conversion. The marginal yield gain from higher pressure is small (simulation: only +2.8 pp from 3→5 atm) and does not justify the cost. ✓
  • High-pressure compressors, vessels, and pipework are very expensive to build, certify, and maintain — capital costs would far outweigh the marginal yield improvement. ✓
  • (Higher pressure increases explosion and leakage risk, raising safety and insurance costs.) ✓
Question 8 — Compromise Temperature 4 marks
Explain why 430–490 °C balances rate and yield.
✓
Low temperature (e.g. 300 °C): Equilibrium yield is very high (simulation >99% at 300 °C), but the catalyst is insufficiently active — the reaction rate is extremely slow, requiring impractically large catalyst beds.
✓
High temperature (e.g. 600 °C): Reaction rate is fast but equilibrium lies far to the left (simulation: α = 23% at 600 °C). The plant would produce very little SO₃ and high fuel costs from the raised temperature.
✓
Compromise at 430–490 °C: V₂O₅ is sufficiently active; rate is commercially acceptable. Single-pass equilibrium gives ~81% at 450 °C (simulation), but combined with inter-bed cooling in 4 passes, overall conversion reaches ~97%.
✓
Modern Cs-promoted catalysts operate down to ~380 °C (higher Kp, better equilibrium per pass) while remaining kinetically viable — further improving the yield–rate balance.
Question 9 — The V₂O₅ Catalyst 4 marks
(a) Role of the catalyst. (b) Two poisons and one protective design feature.
✓✓
(a) V₂O₅ provides an alternative reaction mechanism with a lower activation energy, greatly increasing the rate at which equilibrium is reached. It does NOT change Kp, the equilibrium position, or the maximum yield — these depend only on T and P. Without the catalyst the reaction is too slow at operating temperature to be commercially viable. ✓✓
✓✓
(b) Poisons (any 2): water/steam; dust and ash particles; arsenic compounds (from impure sulfur); fluorides.
Protective design feature matching a named poison:
• Water → air dried in drying tower using 98% H₂SO₄ desiccant before entering plant. ✓
• Dust/ash → removed by hot gas filter downstream of waste heat boiler. ✓
• Arsenic → molten sulfur purified in melter-settler before combustion. ✓
Question 10 — Kp 4 marks
(a) Write Kp expression. (b) Is Kp larger or smaller at 550 °C than at 450 °C? Explain.
✓
(a) Kp = p²(SO₃) / [p²(SO₂) × p(O₂)]    units: atm−1   (or dimensionless when partial pressures divided by p° = 1 bar)
✓✓✓
(b) Kp is smaller at 550 °C. ✓
The reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0). The van’t Hoff equation: d(ln Kp)/dT = ΔH/(RT²) < 0 when ΔH < 0, so Kp decreases as T increases. ✓
Equivalently by Le Chatelier: higher T disfavours the exothermic direction, shifting equilibrium left, consistent with a smaller Kp. ✓
Simulation: Kp(450 °C) = 189; Kp(550 °C) = 3.52 — confirmed.

Question 13 — 4-pass converter 4 marks
Why does the 4-pass design achieve higher conversion than a single bed at the same inlet T and P?
✓
The converter reaction is strongly exothermic. As gas passes through a catalyst bed it heats up, and the rising temperature shifts the equilibrium left, reducing the maximum achievable conversion. ✓
✓
A single long bed would exit at a high temperature (e.g. ~600 °C) where equilibrium conversion is very poor (α ≈ 23% at 600 °C from simulation). ✓
✓
Inter-bed cooling resets the gas temperature to near 430–450 °C before each successive bed, where the equilibrium is more favourable. Each bed achieves partial conversion approaching its local equilibrium, and four beds together achieve a high cumulative conversion. ✓
✓
Overall plant conversion of ~97% is achieved vs the ~81% single-pass equilibrium value at 450 °C from the simulation. ✓
Question 14 — Optimal conditions for high α 4 marks
✓
To achieve α > 99% in the single-stage simulation: temperature must be reduced to ≤ 320 °C (at any P the simulation provides). At 300 °C and 1.5 atm, α = 99.65%. ✓
✓
Increasing pressure also helps but has a smaller effect — at 5 atm / 450 °C, α only reaches 88.1%. To exceed 99% at 5 atm would still require T ≤ ~370 °C. ✓
✓
Economic practicality: T ≤ 320 °C is not practical with standard V₂O₅ catalyst (insufficient activity; reaction too slow). Modern Cs-promoted catalysts operate at ~380 °C, achieving ~95% per pass and overall ~99.7% (double absorption). ✓
✓
The simulation represents an idealised single equilibrium stage — real converters operate on the kinetic-equilibrium trade-off curve, not at a single equilibrium point. The 4-pass design is the practical engineering solution. ✓
🧮 Section D — Mass Balance Calculations
Basis: 1 000 kg hr−1 liquid sulfur. Stated assumption: α = 0.97. Accept ±2% on all calculated values.
Question 15 1 mark
Molar flow rate of sulfur.
ṅ = ṁ / Mr
ṅ(S) = 1000 kg hr−1 ÷ 32 kg kmol−1 = 31.25 kmol hr−1 ✓
Question 16 2 marks
SO₂ from the burner: molar flow and mass flow.
S + O₂ → SO₂ (1:1 mole ratio)
ṅ(SO₂) = ṅ(S) = 31.25 kmol hr−1 ✓
ṁ(SO₂) = 31.25 × 64 = 2000 kg hr−1 ✓
Question 17 2 marks
Converter output at α = 0.97.
SO₃ formed = ṅ(SO₂) × α
ṅ(SO₃) = 31.25 × 0.97 = 30.3125 kmol hr−1 ✓

Unreacted SO₂ = ṅ(SO₂) × (1 − α)
ṅ(SO₂) out = 31.25 × 0.03 = 0.9375 kmol hr−1 ✓
Question 18 2 marks
H₂SO₄ production rate and % of maximum.
SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄   (1:1 mol ratio)
ṁ(H₂SO₄) = 30.3125 kmol hr−1 × 98 kg kmol−1 = 2970.6 kg hr−1 ✓

Maximum at α = 1.0: 31.25 × 98 = 3062.5 kg hr−1
% of maximum = 2970.6 ÷ 3062.5 × 100 = 97.0% ✓
(Self-check: this equals α × 100% — as expected.)
Question 19 2 marks
Atom economy of the process.
Overall balanced equation: S + 3/2 O₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄
Mr(reactants) = 32 + 48 + 18 = 98
Mr(desired product H₂SO₄) = 98
Atom economy = 98 / 98 × 100 = 100% ✓

Key reasoning: there are NO by-products — all atoms in S, O₂, H₂O end up in H₂SO₄. ✓
Marking note: Award 1 mark for “atom economy = 100%” and 1 mark for “no by-products are formed.” The overall equation requires 3/2 O₂ per mol S (not 1 O₂) — students who notice and correct this earn a bonus mark.
Question 20 2 marks
Air feed rates.
(a) SO₂ = 10% of total converter feed:
ṅ(total gas) = 31.25 kmol hr−1 ÷ 0.10 = 312.5 kmol hr−1 ✓

(b) N₂ = 79% of air (balance of O₂ 21% − O₂ used):
ṅ(N₂) = 312.5 × 0.79 = 246.9 kmol hr−1 ✓
🌍 Section E — Industrial & Environmental Considerations
Question 21 — Tail gas SO₂ 6 marks
(a) Mass flow of tail gas SO₂ (using α = 0.97 basis from Section D):
ṅ(SO₂ tail) = 0.9375 kmol hr−1 (from Q17)
ṁ(SO₂) = 0.9375 × 64 = 60 kg hr−1 ✓
✓✓
(b) Environmental problem — acid rain: SO₂ dissolves in atmospheric moisture and oxidises to give sulphurous acid (H₂SO₃) and sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), causing acid rain (pH 4–5, sometimes below 3). Effects: corrosion of limestone and marble buildings; acidification of lakes, rivers and soils; forest damage; human respiratory irritant. ✓✓
✓✓✓✓
(c) Double absorption:
• After the 3rd catalyst pass (~90% SO₂ converted), the gas is diverted through an intermediate absorber where SO₃ is removed into sulphuric acid. ✓
• The remaining gas (SO₂, O₂, N₂) is returned for the 4th catalyst pass. ✓
• Removing SO₃ product shifts the equilibrium of the 4th pass strongly right (Le Chatelier), allowing much higher conversion in that final pass. ✓
• Overall SO₂ conversion rises from ~97% (single absorption) to ~99.7% (double absorption), reducing tail gas SO₂ by a factor of ~10. ✓
Question 22 — Heat recovery economics 4 marks
(a) Annual steam revenue:
Steam rate = 1500 kg hr−1 = 1.5 t hr−1
Annual output = 1.5 × 8000 hr yr−1 = 12 000 t yr−1
Revenue = 12 000 × £15 = £180 000 yr−1 ✓ (accept ±10%)
✓✓✓
(b) Gas-gas HX economic benefit: Without the HX, the converter feed gas would need to be heated from near ambient to ~430 °C using external fuel, adding significant operating cost. The HX transfers heat already present in the hot converter exit gas (~550 °C) to perform this pre-heating for free. ✓   In steady operation, the Contact Process can be nearly thermally self-sufficient — the heat of reaction heats the feed of the next cycle. ✓   This reduces both operating cost and carbon footprint compared to external heating. ✓

Question 24 — Extension: Sustainable improvements 3 marks — 1 per valid point

Accept any reasonable suggestion supported by chemical/engineering reasoning. Model answers:

✓
Cs-promoted catalysts: Operate at ~380 °C (vs 430 °C), giving higher equilibrium conversion per pass while remaining kinetically active — reduces both energy use and SO₂ emissions.
✓
Waste sulfur feedstocks: Using sulfur recovered from oil and gas desulphurisation (Claus process) as feedstock converts a waste product into a raw material, reducing the environmental footprint of sulfur extraction.
✓
Mandatory double absorption: Reduces tail gas SO₂ from ~60 kg hr−1 to <6 kg hr−1, almost eliminating the acid rain contribution from the plant.
✓
Net electricity export: Advanced heat integration (pinch analysis) can optimise the steam system to export electricity, making the plant a net energy producer rather than consumer.
✓
Tail gas scrubbing with ammonia: Residual SO₂ reacts with NH₃ to give ammonium sulphate — a valuable N/S fertiliser — converting a pollutant into a product.
