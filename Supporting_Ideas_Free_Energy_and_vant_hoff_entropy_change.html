Van 't Hoff Equation & Entropy Change Conversation

Student:

What is the Van't Hoff equation and what does it have to do with Entropy Change?

Guide:

The van 't Hoff equation is a fundamental formula in chemical thermodynamics that describes how the chemical equilibrium constant (K) of a reaction changes with variations in temperature (T).

Here is how it connects directly to the total entropy change of the universe and the standard entropy change of the reaction.

1. The Van 't Hoff Equation

In its differential form, the van 't Hoff equation is written as:

d(ln K) / dT = ΔH° / (R T2)

Where:

  • K is the equilibrium constant.
  • T is the absolute temperature (in Kelvin).
  • ΔH° is the standard enthalpy change of the reaction.
  • R is the ideal gas constant.

Notice this differential form isn’t something you could plot directly as a straight line — but if we make one simplifying assumption, it becomes something very familiar. If we treat ΔH° and the standard entropy change (ΔS°) as roughly constant over the temperature range we’re working in, we can integrate the equation into its more useful integrated form:

ln K = -(ΔH° / R) × (1 / T) + (ΔS° / R)

Look closely and you’ll recognise the shape of a straight-line equation, y = mx + b. If you plot ln K (the y-axis) against 1/T (the x-axis), you should get a straight line whose gradient is -ΔH°/R and whose y-intercept is ΔS°/R. This is exactly the same trick you already used for the Arrhenius Plot in Kinetics (ln k against 1/T, gradient -Ea/R) — the van 't Hoff plot is really that same idea, just applied to an equilibrium constant instead of a rate constant.

A word of caution: the dashed straight line above is only an approximation. It was drawn by assuming ΔH° (and ΔS°) stay perfectly constant as temperature changes — in reality they drift slightly, which is why real (1/T, ln K) data usually traces a gentle curve rather than a perfectly straight line, especially towards the ends of your measured range. The gradient and intercept you extract are therefore best estimates over the temperature range you actually tested, not exact values valid at all temperatures — including at 1/T = 0 (infinite temperature!), where the y-intercept is technically extrapolated to and can never really be measured directly.

2. The Relationship to Entropy Change

The connection between the van 't Hoff equation and entropy is deep, rooted in the Second Law of Thermodynamics. We can look at this relationship through two lenses: system entropy (ΔS°) and total entropy (ΔStotal).

A. The Y-Intercept Represents System Entropy (ΔS°)

Look back at the integrated equation and the graph above: the constant of integration — the term that doesn’t depend on temperature at all — is directly proportional to the standard entropy change of the system:

Intercept = ΔS° / R

In practice, this means that if you measure the equilibrium constant of a reaction at several different temperatures, convert each temperature to 1/T, and plot ln K against 1/T as shown above, the point where your best-fit line would cross the y-axis (at 1/T = 0, i.e. an unreachable, theoretically infinite temperature) tells you ΔS° — how much disorder or freedom the molecules gain or lose during the reaction.

B. Connection to Total Entropy and Spontaneity

The ultimate driving force for any process is an increase in the total entropy of the universe (ΔStotal > 0). The total entropy change is the sum of the system's entropy change and the surroundings' entropy change:

ΔStotal = ΔSsystem + ΔSsurroundings

We know that heat transferred to the surroundings at a constant pressure changes the surroundings' entropy by ΔSsurroundings = -ΔH° / T. Substituting this in:

ΔStotal = ΔS° - (ΔH° / T)

If we divide this entire equation by the gas constant R, we get:

ΔStotal / R = -(ΔH° / RT) + (ΔS° / R)

Notice how the right side of this equation perfectly matches the right side of the integrated van 't Hoff equation! Therefore:

ln K = ΔStotal / R

Summary

The van 't Hoff equation is essentially a map of how temperature balances the two components of total entropy:

  • At low temperatures, the enthalpy term (-ΔH° / RT) dominates, meaning the heat released to the surroundings (surroundings' entropy) dictates the equilibrium.
  • At high temperatures, the 1/T factor shrinks the enthalpy term, allowing the system's internal entropy change (ΔS° / R) to dominate the equilibrium.
Student:

I'm measuring the change of a standard electrode potential with changing temperature. How will Van 't Hoff help me here, if I would like to estimate the ∆S of a reaction?

Guide:

To find the standard entropy change (ΔS°) of a redox reaction using temperature-dependent electrochemical data, you are actually standing right at the intersection of the van 't Hoff equation and the Nernst equation.

While the classic van 't Hoff equation uses the equilibrium constant (K), you can easily adapt it for standard cell potentials (E°). Here is how the math connects and how you can use your data to estimate ΔS°.

1. Connecting Cell Potential to van 't Hoff

The link between electrochemistry and thermodynamics relies on two fundamental equations that equal the standard Gibbs free energy change (ΔG°):

  1. Electrochemistry: ΔG° = -nFE°
  2. Thermodynamics: ΔG° = -RT ln K

(Where n is the number of moles of electrons transferred, and F is Faraday's constant, 96,485 C/mol.)

By setting these equal to each other, we find that ln K = nFE° / RT. If we substitute this directly into the integrated van 't Hoff equation:

nFE° / RT = -(ΔH° / RT) + (ΔS° / R)

Multiplying the entire equation by RT / nF isolates the standard cell potential:

E° = -(ΔH° / nF) + (ΔS° / nF)T

2. How to Estimate ΔS° From Your Data

If you assume ΔH° and ΔS° stay relatively constant over your measured temperature range, the equation above takes the form of a straight line (y = mx + b).

Method A: The Linear Plot (The van 't Hoff Approach)

  1. Measure your standard electrode potential (E°) at several different temperatures (T in Kelvin).
  2. Plot E° on the y-axis against T on the x-axis.
  3. Fit a straight line to your data.

According to our formula, the slope (m) of this line is directly related to your entropy change:

Slope (m) = ΔS° / nF

Therefore, you can calculate the standard entropy change simply by multiplying the slope by n and F:

ΔS° = nF × Slope
¡ Note on Units: Because E° is in Volts (J/C) and T is in Kelvin, your slope will have units of V/K. Multiplying by Faraday's constant (C/mol) yields J·K-1·mol-1, which are the perfect standard units for entropy.

Method B: The Temperature Coefficient Shortcut

If you look at the differential calculus behind this relationship, the fundamental thermodynamic equation for entropy is:

ΔS° = -(∂ΔG° / ∂T)P

Substituting ΔG° = -nFE° gives you the standard electrochemical shortcut:

ΔS° = nF(dE° / dT)

The term dE°/dT is known as the temperature coefficient of the cell. If your plot of E° vs. T is linear, dE°/dT is simply the slope of that line.
