Q3 The Role of the Recycle Stream

Stream ⑥ is a recycle stream that returns concentrated sulphuric acid from the diluter back to the absorbers. Explain why this recycle is necessary rather than adding water directly to absorb the SO₃.

💡 Hint
What happens physically when SO₃ gas meets liquid water? Think about the energy released and the state of the product formed.
📋 Model answer
If SO₃ were absorbed directly by water, the highly exothermic reaction SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ would generate so much heat that the water vaporises instantly, and the H₂SO₄ forms as a fine aerosol mist of submicron droplets. These are too small to be collected and escape as a visible acid plume — a serious pollutant. By recycling concentrated (98%) H₂SO₄ instead, the SO₃ dissolves into the acid without misting. The 98% acid (not 100%) is critical — 100% acid also causes misting.

Q5 The V₂O₅ Catalyst

The converter uses vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) as a catalyst.

(a) What does the catalyst do to the reaction rate and to the equilibrium position?

(b) V₂O₅ has a minimum working temperature of about 400 °C. What would you observe in the simulation if you set the temperature below 400 °C, and why does this limit matter in practice?

💡 Hint
Try moving the temperature slider below 400°C in the simulation. Look at what the Le Chatelier box says about reaction rate. Remember: equilibrium conversion shown by the model is a thermodynamic limit — in reality the catalyst would be too inactive to reach it in a reasonable time.
📋 Model answer
(a) The catalyst increases the rate at which equilibrium is reached by providing an alternative reaction mechanism with a lower activation energy. It has no effect on the equilibrium position or the maximum yield — these depend only on temperature and pressure.

(b) In the simulation, moving below 400°C gives very high conversion (thermodynamically excellent) but the "Reaction rate" indicator switches to "Very slow ⚠". In practice, below ~400°C V₂O₅ is insufficiently active — the reaction proceeds so slowly that reaching equilibrium would require impractically large catalyst beds. The plant would be uneconomic.

(c) Does Kp increase or decrease as temperature rises? Explain using the van’t Hoff equation or Le Chatelier’s Principle.

📋 Model answers
(a) Kp = p²(SO₃) / [p²(SO₂) × p(O₂)]    units: atm−1

(b) Kp(350°C) ≈ 36258  |  Kp(450°C) ≈ 189  |  Kp(550°C) ≈ 3.5

(c) Kp decreases as temperature increases. The reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0), so by Le Chatelier’s Principle, increasing T favours the endothermic reverse reaction, shifting equilibrium left and reducing Kp. Mathematically, the van’t Hoff equation d(ln Kp)/dT = ΔH/RT² < 0 when ΔH < 0.

Q8 Compromise Temperature

The converter operates at 430–490 °C even though lower temperatures would give a higher equilibrium yield of SO₃. Explain why this temperature range is chosen as a compromise.

💡 Hint
Move the temperature slider from 300°C to 600°C. Watch both the conversion % AND the reaction rate indicator. What happens to each at low temperature? At high temperature? A good answer addresses both extremes.
📋 Model answer
At low temperatures (<350°C): the equilibrium position is very favourable (high Kp, high conversion shown by the simulation) but the catalyst is too inactive — the rate is very slow. The plant would need enormous catalyst beds and would be uneconomic.

At high temperatures (>550°C): the reaction rate is fast, but Kp is small and equilibrium lies far to the left — conversion is poor.

The compromise at 430–490°C gives acceptable reaction rate with the catalyst while still achieving reasonable equilibrium conversion. In a real 4-pass converter, inter-bed cooling between catalyst beds allows each pass to approach equilibrium at a cooler, more favourable temperature, boosting overall conversion above what any single stage could achieve.

Q13 What is Missing from this Simulation?

This simplified simulation shows only 5 unit operations. Real industrial Contact Process plants contain several additional items not shown here. Use your knowledge and the simulation to suggest three units or features that are missing and explain why each matters.

💡 Hint
Think about: what happens to the air before it enters the furnace? The furnace produces very hot gas (~1000°C) — what should happen to this heat? The stream leaving the converter still contains unreacted SO₂ — what happens to it in a real plant? What purification does the sulfur need?
📋 Model answers (any 3)
1. Drying tower: Air must be dried (e.g. by contact with 98% H₂SO₄) before entering the furnace. Water vapour poisons the V₂O₅ catalyst and causes acid mist downstream.
2. Waste heat boiler: Combustion gases from the furnace exit at ~1000°C. A waste heat boiler generates steam for energy recovery, cooling the gas to the converter inlet temperature and improving plant economics.
3. Gas-gas heat exchanger: Hot converter exit gas pre-heats the cold converter feed gas, avoiding the need for external fuel heating and making the process nearly self-heating.
4. Hot gas filter: Removes dust and particulate matter from the SO₂ stream before the converter, protecting the catalyst from physical poisoning.
5. Tail gas scrubber / double absorption: The absorber off-gas still contains unreacted SO₂, which must not be released to atmosphere (acid rain). Double absorption removes most of this.
6. Melter-settler for sulfur: Real plants purify and melt solid sulfur feedstock before combustion, removing arsenic and other catalyst poisons.

📋 Model answers
(a) ṁ(SO₂) = ṅ(SO₂ tail) × M_r(SO₂)
= 6.25 × 64 = 400 kg hr⁻¹ ✓
(b) SO₂ dissolves in atmospheric moisture to form sulphurous acid (H₂SO₃) and oxidises to sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), causing acid rain. Q22 Evaluating the Contact Process

Using what you have learned from this worksheet and the simulation, write a short evaluation (4–6 sentences) of the Contact Process. Your answer should address: atom economy, yield, environmental impact, and the rate–yield compromise.

💡 Prompt questions to help you
• What are the strengths of the Contact Process in terms of green chemistry?
• What are its limitations or environmental concerns?
• Why can’t you simply run at the most thermodynamically favourable conditions?
• What engineering compromises are made, and why?
📋 Model answer
The Contact Process has a theoretical atom economy of 100% — an outstanding green chemistry metric, as all raw material atoms end up in the product. In practice, equilibrium conversion of 77–81% at typical conditions (450°C, 1–2 atm) means around 20% of the SO₂ feedstock exits unreacted as a pollutant that causes acid rain, so environmental controls such as double absorption and tail gas scrubbing are essential. The process faces a fundamental rate-yield compromise: lower temperatures dramatically improve equilibrium yield (as shown by the simulation heatmap) but slow the reaction to impractical rates below ~400°C. The V₂O₅ catalyst allows operation at 430–490°C where both yield and rate are acceptable. Modern improvements including Cs-promoted catalysts (lower minimum T) and inter-bed cooling (4-pass converter) have pushed overall plant conversions above 97%, greatly reducing environmental impact. The integration of waste heat recovery (not shown in this simulation) makes the real process nearly energy self-sufficient.
