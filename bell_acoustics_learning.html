Bell Acoustics — A Learning Sequence

Based on W. A. Hibbert (2008) — The Quantification of Strike Pitch and Pitch Shifts in Church Bells

Stage 1 — The anatomy of a bell's sound

Almost all musical instruments produce harmonic sounds: frequencies at f, 2f, 3f, 4f… whose pitch we assign to f. Bell sounds are different: their frequencies are inharmonic — they do not fit a neat whole-number pattern. Each distinct frequency component is called a partial.

The five lowest partials (hum, prime, tierce, quint, nominal) have been known and named for centuries. The upper partials (superquint upward) are less visible but — as the later stages will show — are the primary drivers of the pitch you actually hear.

Stage 2 — What note do we hear? The virtual pitch paradox

Here is the paradox at the heart of bell acoustics: the note you hear when a bell strikes is often not present as any frequency in the bell's sound. This phenomenon — known as virtual pitch or the missing fundamental — was confirmed in bells by experiments from the late 1920s onwards, but its practical consequences for bellfounders had not been recognised when Hibbert began his PhD in 2002.

"Pitches of bells are generally virtual pitch or missing fundamental effects, generated in the ear rather than present as a frequency."
Demonstrate the missing fundamental. The three upper partials — nominal, superquint and octave nominal — are close to a 4:6:8 harmonic ratio, which the auditory system interprets as evidence of a fundamental one octave below the nominal. Hear them alone, then compare against that reference tone.

💡 The dominance region (500–1600 Hz nominal): For bells in this range — roughly 500 kg to 2 tonnes — most listeners hear a strike pitch generated by the upper partials, about an octave below the nominal. For large bells (nominal below ~500 Hz) a secondary strike from partial I-7 competes or dominates. For small bells (above ~1600 Hz) the prime or hum take over.

The practical implication: for the majority of bells in a typical peal, the exact pitch heard depends on the spacing of the upper partials — not just the nominal alone. Hibbert's key contribution was to quantify this dependency.

Stage 3 — Upper partials cannot be tuned independently

Hibbert analysed 2,066 bells (later extended to over 8,000) from 15 countries spanning 700 years — English, Flemish, Dutch, German, French, Italian, Austrian and beyond. 📖 Case study — The Berlin Freedom Bell (Gillett & Johnston, 1950)

G&J attempted to tune the upper partials of an 11-tonne bell to exact musical intervals. They removed 1.4 tonnes of metal on the tuning machine. The result confirmed Hibbert's finding directly: all upper partials moved together along the regression lines — independent tuning was impossible. The hum ended up 53¢ flat; the octave nominal 71¢ sharp; the sound was worse than before.

💡 The key result: Every upper partial correlates tightly with every other (R² typically >0.95) — and this holds across every tradition on the chart: English, Flemish, German, French, Italian, Austrian. The scatter shows that Italian bells cluster at the sharp end (thin walls, widely-spaced partials) while Historic English bells cluster at the flat end (thick walls, compressed partials) — but all lie on the same regression line. The single parameter controlling them all is the octave nominal interval. Bellfounders can move it up or down (by thinning/thickening near the soundbow), but all upper partials shift together in exact proportion. Independent tuning of superquint, I-7, I-8 etc. is physically impossible in a bell of any tradition or date.

Stage 4 — Quantifying the pitch shift

Listening experiments with 30 subjects confirmed: thick-walled bells compress upper partials, flattening perceived pitch; thin bells spread them, sharpening it. This had been observed empirically by bell tuners for centuries — but never quantified. Hibbert derived a regression model from the experimental data.

"The results of this experiment allow, for the first time, the shift in pitch to be calculated."

The octave-nominal term dominates, accounting for ~60% of pitch-shift variance at 1000 Hz. At nominal = 300 Hz, a ±150¢ spread change shifts pitch by ±40¢ — over a third of a semitone. At nominal = 2000 Hz, the same change produces only ±10¢. This frequency dependence is why the dominance region (Stage 2) matters so much.

"…showed that partial amplitude does not significantly affect the strike pitch." Volume differences between partials matter for timbre, not for the note heard.

Stage 5 — Stretch tuning explained and validated

In peals, experienced bellfounders tune treble bells sharp relative to equal temperament. This practice — called stretch tuning — had never been formally explained. Hibbert's model predicts it exactly: thick-walled treble bells have compressed upper partials which flatten perceived pitch; sharpening the nominal corrects for this. 💡 Validation: Hibbert tested the model against six real peals. In every case, model predictions matched actual foundry tuning to within a few cents, with R² between 0.80 and 0.98. Stretch tuning is not a matter of taste — it is a physical correction for partial-compression-induced pitch flattening in thick treble bells. The bass bells are often tuned slightly flat, for the same reason in reverse.

"…the minor discrepancies are due to tuning errors rather than deficiencies in the model."

Stage 6 — Sound quality: which partials matter, and when?

Different partials shape a bell's sound quality depending on its size. This brings together all previous findings into a practical guide. Select a bell size to see which partials are significant, which are secondary, and which can be ignored.

"The most important factor is the relationship of the various physical partial frequencies to the dominant strike pitch."
Bell size (by nominal frequency):
Secondary strike and true-harmonic tuning

For large bells (nominal < 500 Hz), I-7 creates a secondary strike roughly a fourth above the primary. The interval between primary and secondary strikes is what gives large bells their characteristic sound — in thicker bells this interval narrows toward a major third, which can be appealing; in thinner bells it widens to a sharp fourth, which can sound unpleasant.

For medium and small bells, true-harmonic tuning (hum, prime and nominal in exact octaves) produces the clearest, most harmonious sound. However, even a true-harmonic bell will sound flat and dull if the upper partials are compressed — because the virtual pitch no longer matches the physical hum and prime frequencies.

💡 Summary of the full research sequence:
1. Bell pitch is a virtual pitch generated in the ear, not a physical frequency in the sound.
2. Which partial drives that virtual pitch depends on bell size (the dominance region).
3. All upper partials are linearly correlated — independent tuning is impossible.
4. Upper partial spacing shifts perceived pitch by up to half a semitone — now quantified.
5. This quantified shift exactly explains stretch tuning in real peals.
6. Sound quality across the full range of bell sizes is now predictable from partial frequencies alone.

Glossary & Key Phrases

Campanology

Peal
A matched set of tuned church bells (typically 6–12) designed to be rung together in change ringing.
Bellfounder
A craftsman or foundry responsible for casting and initially tuning metal bells.
True-harmonic tuning
A modern tuning method where the five lowest partials (hum, prime, tierce, quint, nominal) are ground to exact musical intervals relative to each other. Bells tuned this way have hum, prime and nominal in exact octaves.
Stretch tuning / Stretched peal
The practice of tuning treble bells progressively sharper than equal temperament to compensate for the flattened virtual pitch caused by compressed upper partials in thick-walled bells. Hibbert's research proved this is a physical necessity, not an aesthetic choice.
Bell profile / Wall thickness
The cross-sectional shape of a bell. Thicker walls produce compressed upper partials; thinner walls produce wider-spaced upper partials.

Physical & Acoustic Terms

Partials / Partial tones
The individual component frequencies that make up a complex sound. A bell's sound comprises many simultaneous partials. Also called overtones or modes of vibration.
Inharmonic
A sound whose component frequencies do not fit a neat harmonic series (f, 2f, 3f, 4f…). Bell partials are inharmonic, which is why the ear cannot simply identify the lowest frequency as the pitch.
Nominal
The fifth partial of a bell (counting upward by frequency). The nominal's frequency gives the bell its note name. The virtual strike pitch heard is roughly one octave below the nominal.
Hum
The lowest partial of a bell, roughly two octaves below the nominal. In a true-harmonic bell it is tuned to be exactly two octaves below the nominal.
Octave nominal (ON)
The upper partial approximately one octave above the nominal. Its interval relative to the nominal characterises the "spread" of all upper partials. Hibbert showed it is a proxy for all other upper partials.
Cent / Semitone
Units of musical interval. A semitone is the step between two adjacent piano keys. A cent is 1/100 of a semitone — used to track small pitch differences. 100 cents = 1 semitone; 1200 cents = 1 octave.
Chladni's Law
A mathematical formula relating vibration frequencies to the number of nodal lines in symmetric structures. Modified versions apply approximately to bells, but Hibbert found a better linear fit for upper partials.

Psychoacoustic Terms

Virtual pitch / Missing fundamental
A pitch sensation generated in the auditory system when the corresponding physical frequency is absent from the sound. The ear infers it from the spacing of higher harmonics. The strike pitch of a bell is a virtual pitch.
Missing fundamental effect
Another name for virtual pitch. The "fundamental" is the note the ear hears even though no physical frequency at that pitch is present in the sound wave.
Strike pitch / Strike note
The principal musical note perceived when a bell is struck. For most bells, it is a virtual pitch approximately one octave below the nominal.
Secondary strike
A second, higher pitch perceived in large bells (nominal below ~500 Hz), generated by the I-7 partial group via virtual pitch mechanisms. Approximately a fourth above the primary strike.
Dominance region
The frequency band (~500–1600 Hz) where the human ear preferentially relies on virtual pitch from upper partials. Bells whose upper partials fall in this range are most affected by partial spacing changes.
JND — Just Noticeable Difference
The smallest pitch difference reliably detected by a listener. Hibbert's experiments showed human JND for bells is approximately ±10 cents. Shifts larger than this are musically significant.
Spectral pitch
Pitch identified by listening to a single physical frequency, rather than constructing a virtual pitch. Becomes more important in very small bells where upper partials are above the dominance region.

Key Academic Phrases

"The primary objective of the work reported in this thesis was to quantify…"

Announces the research aim precisely — to produce a numerical model, not just a qualitative description.

"Pitches of bells are generally virtual pitch or missing fundamental effects, generated in the ear rather than present as a frequency…"

Establishes the foundational premise: what we hear is a cognitive construct, not a physical wave.

"An unexpected and straightforward relationship was found between…"

Signals a major discovery made during data analysis — the linear correlation of upper partials.

"The relative frequencies of these partials are in turn determined by…"

Establishes a chain of physical causality: wall thickness → partial spacing → perceived pitch.

"…explains previous failed attempts by bellfounders to…"

Connects abstract science to real historical industry events (e.g. the Berlin Freedom Bell).

"…did not predict the shifts seen in practice."

Used to show that existing algorithms (e.g. Terhardt's) failed to model the measured phenomenon.

"…showed that partial amplitude does not significantly affect…"

Isolates one variable (loudness) to show it does not drive the effect — pitch shift comes from frequency spacing, not volume.

"…was found not to give as good a fit to their frequencies as the relationship discovered in this research."

Standard comparative phrasing showing the new model outperforms historic formulas such as Chladni's Law.
