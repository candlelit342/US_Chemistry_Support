Why does Le Chatelier's Principle actually work? (particle-level explanation)

Equilibrium is dynamic, not static. At equilibrium, N₂, H₂ and NH₃ molecules are still colliding and reacting constantly — the forward rate (N₂ + H₂ → NH₃) and reverse rate (NH₃ → N₂ + H₂) are simply equal, so concentrations stop changing on average even though individual reactions never stop.

Collision theory says a reaction needs two things: a collision, and enough energy in that collision to exceed the activation energy (Eₐ), plus the correct molecular orientation. The fraction of collisions with enough energy is set by the Boltzmann distribution at a given temperature — this is exactly what the "success probability" in this simulation represents.

Temperature ↑: particles move faster, so collisions happen more often and a bigger fraction of them clear the energy barrier — both rates increase. But because the reverse reaction (endothermic here) has a higher activation energy than the forward reaction (exothermic), the reverse rate is more sensitive to temperature and grows proportionally more. Net effect: the reverse rate temporarily exceeds the forward rate, NH₃ is consumed faster than it forms, and the system shifts left — until a new, lower-NH₃ balance point is reached where the rates are equal again. This is the particle-level reason "increasing T shifts an exothermic equilibrium toward reactants."

Pressure ↑ (volume ↓): squeezing the same number of molecules into a smaller space increases the local density of every species. The forward reaction needs a chance encounter of 1 N₂ with 3 H₂ molecules close together — a much less probable event than the 2-NH₃ encounter needed for the reverse reaction. Compression raises the odds of these crowded, multi-molecule encounters much more steeply for the forward direction, so the forward rate rises faster than the reverse rate. The system shifts right (more NH₃) — consistent with Le Chatelier, but here it emerges directly from collision statistics rather than being a rule to memorize: the side with fewer gas molecules (2 NH₃ vs. 4 total reactant molecules) is favored by compression because it requires a less demanding simultaneous encounter.

Adding/removing a species: add more H₂, and N₂ molecules simply encounter enough nearby H₂ far more often — the forward rate jumps immediately while the reverse rate hasn't changed yet, so net production of NH₃ occurs until the rates re-equalize. Remove NH₃, and reverse collisions become rarer, so forward reaction dominates until balance is restored. In every case, the "shift" you see is just the forward and reverse rates temporarily going out of balance and then re-equalizing at a new set of concentrations.

Catalyst: a catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy for both directions (see the energy diagram) — but for this specific reaction, its bigger job is making the forward encounter geometrically achievable in the first place. Synthesizing NH₃ means breaking N₂'s very strong triple bond, which needs 1 N₂ and 3 H₂ to meet in just the right orientation at once — without a catalyst surface to hold and align them, that almost never happens, no matter how hot you make it, so forward synthesis is essentially switched off. Decomposition (2NH₃ → N₂ + 3H₂) doesn't need that same precise alignment, so it keeps happening — slowly at low temperature, faster as T rises — with or without a catalyst. Insert the catalyst and forward synthesis becomes possible again, so equilibrium (with a real NH₃ concentration) is reached quickly. This is a simplification: true thermodynamics (ΔH and the equilibrium constant K) never depends on a catalyst — but without one, this reaction's kinetics are so lopsided that the forward direction is, for all practical purposes, unreachable, so you won't see the system settle into the same equilibrium at all within any observable time.

Educational model. Reaction probabilities follow an Arrhenius-style e^(−Eₐ/kT) relationship with realistic relative trends (reverse Eₐ > forward Eₐ by |ΔH|; catalyst lowers both Eₐ values equally). Without the catalyst, forward synthesis is additionally modeled as needing a near-impossible molecular alignment (a very low orientation/steric factor), keeping it negligible at every simulated temperature, while reverse decomposition uses a fixed, more forgiving orientation factor whether or not the catalyst is present — reflecting that only synthesis strictly requires the catalytic surface to proceed at any observable rate. Energy values are in arbitrary simulation units, not real kJ/mol. Real Haber-process ΔH ≈ −92 kJ/mol; real uncatalyzed Eₐ is very high due to the strong N≡N triple bond, which is why an iron catalyst is essential industrially.
